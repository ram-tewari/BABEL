Chapter 1206:
Fermi had begun to grasp the essence of Guitar Man but refrained from
hastily forming an image of him.
"I heard he lost his daughter."
"It was a foolish act," Pannier replied. "That guy truly
loved his daughter. He had no malice. He just... didn't know any better. He had never lived properly in
the real world, so he had no idea how to handle things."
Pannier let out a deep sigh.
"After that, his wife ran away with some guy who had been
Lanstein's friend for ten years. So, how could he possibly stay sane? Guys like Lanstein are all the
same. They always end up getting the short end of the stick, all for the sake of their so-called
artistic soul. I can't even blame the guy who ran off with his wife—it's not like what he did was
unforgivable. But still, is that something a person should do? Then again, maybe he did it because he's
human. I always felt like that guy had an inferiority complex toward Lanstein. He was never really fond
of him."
Unlike when he had spoken in front of Maya, Pannier didn't bother hiding
his emotions now.
"On the other hand, you liked Lanstein, didn't you?"
Pannier remained silent for a moment before glancing at Fermi as he
pulled out a cigarette.
"If you don't mind..."
"Please, give me one as well."
As he lit his cigarette, Pannier seemed to relax.
"There was a time when I went around shouting that I was an artist,
too. Not that I was particularly glamorous or successful. Fame, popularity—those are all illusions of
show business."
He smirked, reminiscing about the past.
"I used to hole myself up in a tiny room, downing cheap liquor
while scribbling notes on sheet music all day. I was obsessed with creating a masterpiece that would
transcend time. Heh. But you know, I was free. People always say you can't do anything without money,
but it's actually the opposite. When you have nothing, your soul is free. You do whatever you want. I
wrote the most bizarre compositions back then. Of course, nobody ever listened to them. But still... how
do I put it?"
A stream of smoke drifted toward the ceiling.
"It was the most me I had ever been."
"Do you dislike your life now?"
"I mean, it's still a good life. You saw how those artists couldn't
even argue back with me, didn't you?"
But his expression darkened slightly.
"Still, just because things turned out well doesn't mean I can
justify how I got here. I got into this because I wanted to live life as an artist. I wanted to shock
the world. But it wasn't that easy. I worked for over ten years to provide for my kids—I'm divorced now,
by the way. While Lanstein was making his art, I was out there working."
He spread his arms.
"Which one of us had it better? Obviously, I did. I worked hard and
protected my family. But I can't deny that I had less courage—that I compromised with life."
Pannier never called himself an artist.
"It would've been nice if things had worked out for him. If he had
just compromised a little, if he had mixed just a little bit of falsehood into the truth, people would
have applauded him. But he never did. He would rather beg for money than beg for applause."
"I see," Fermi murmured, lost in thought.
'Kayden's escape, Maya's pursuit of her best effort, and Guitar
Man…'
No compromise.
The picture of the future was becoming clearer.
Pannier asked, "By the way, do you know what he's up to these days?
I don't think he's released any albums."
Fermi couldn't exactly tell him that Guitar Man, Lanstein, was one of
the world's 100 most dangerous individuals.
"I have no idea. That's why I came to you. Do you have any
guesses?"
"Hmm. I can think of a few places. Inspiration tends to come from
certain environments. But if he's still playing the guitar..."
Pannier took another drag from his cigarette.
"Wherever he is, he's living artistically."
No one knew the name of the city.
Ever since leaving his hometown, every place in the world had been
unfamiliar to Guitar Man.
In an unknown inn, he sat quietly, listening to the sounds of a man and
a woman breathing through the walls.
One of those voices belonged to Tyra Lyn, his exclusive singer.
It wasn't as if they couldn't afford a room and had to resort to
prostitution.
It was simply that there was no reason not to.
'Just going with the flow.'
As if trying to prove with his entire being that he was disconnected
from the world.
Meanwhile, Lyn was getting irritated.
"Hurry up and finish."
She had a youthful face and a petite frame, but in reality, she was
nearing her thirties.
The man she had just been with asked, "You're really something. Do
you work here?"
"No."
Buttoning up her shirt, Lyn answered, "I don't belong anywhere.
After tomorrow's performance, I'm leaving this city. So don't ask me anything."
"A performance? What kind of performance?"
She pulled out a ticket with the time and location written on it.
"If you want to come, then come."
The man, looking slightly dazed, tucked the ticket into his pocket
before asking again.
"You're a singer? Then why are you... No, wait—should I even be
going?"
Lyn smirked.
"That's up to you. I just sing wherever I go."
After collecting her payment, she left the room without saying goodbye
and entered the next one.
"I'm back."
Guitar Man, Rove Lanstein, didn't ask any questions.
"I wrote a new song."
Lyn tossed the money onto the table and collapsed onto the bed, flipping
through the sheet music.
"Not bad."
Guitar Man resumed playing his instrument.
They had gone four years without speaking due to differences in their
musical philosophies, and even after reconciling, they still didn't talk much.
If he hadn't been afflicted with the emotion plague, Lanstein would have
remained just as silent.
'Stubborn fool. Idiot.'
Among all the people Lyn knew, Rove Lanstein was the most uncompromising
artist.
"I like it."
That was exactly why she followed him.
"I'll sing it for you."
Lanstein picked up his object, Guitaru, and began playing the piece he
had just finished.
The name Guitaru likely meant "guitar," yet it resonated with
the sounds of every instrument at once.
Lyn hummed along as she sang.
Had an audience been present, they might have frowned at the lyrics—yet
the sorrowful melody carried them away.
The next afternoon, Lanstein and Lyn arrived at the square, paid the
rental fee, and booked a small theater for about an hour.
Since they didn't require a full session, the setup was quick, and soon,
a decent crowd had gathered.
Most of them were people Lyn had been acquainted with over the past two
weeks.
"Huh?"
The only one who recognized her true self was Kinsler, a composer who
had once attended the same arts university as her.
"Tyra Lyn?"
Perhaps his first love.
But back then, everyone at school—regardless of gender—was enamored with
Lyn.
Her otherworldly youthful appearance was striking, but it was her voice
that truly set hearts racing among all aspiring artists.
'She was an incredible singer.'
Kinsler had heard rumors about why she dropped out, but even knowing
them, it was hard to believe.
'It was a barbaric society.'
That school had gathered the strangest and most eccentric people in one
place, and with that came countless incidents and scandals.
"Hello, I'll be singing now."
With a simple greeting, Lyn took the microphone, and Lanstein began a
dazzling performance on his instrument.
"Whoo! Whoo!"
The men in the audience whistled and cheered wildly, making Kinsler
click his tongue.
As the song's lyrics turned decadent and provocative, the crowd's
enthusiasm only grew louder.
No one realized it.
That with a single note from her voice, with a single flick of
Lanstein's fingers, everyone in the square could die.
"Is it her?"
"Yeah, I told you, last night I—"
Hearing snippets of conversation among the audience during the
interlude, Kinsler's face burned with embarrassment.
'What the hell are they saying?'
As crude and explicit jokes stretched on, he could no longer hold back
his anger.
"Hey, cut it out! What do you gain from slandering a singer like
this?"
"Who the hell are you?"
"Do you even know who she is? If you keep this up, you could be
sued."
"Pfft, sued? Who even is she? I was with her last—"
Lyn continued to sing.
"I'm gonna kiss that woman walking down 73rd Street. Maybe then
this shitty feeling will go away. But you know what? It doesn't even matter anymore. It's not like I'm
the one who ruined everything."
As Kinsler took in the lyrics, his mind went blank.
'That can't be right.'
Someone with this much talent, with such a breathtaking performance—why
would she have thrown her life away?
When the performance ended, the crowd swarmed in.
Most of them, of course, had stayed for the wrong reasons, but Lyn
treated them all with polite courtesy.
'Don't ruin my memories of her.'
The girl he couldn't even dare to look at back in school was now
enduring these kinds of crude advances.
"Thank you! Thank you so much!"
After enduring a few jokes and unwanted touches, Lyn cheerfully packed
up her equipment.
"We had a decent turnout today, right?"
Lanstein remained silent.
"What? What's with the long face again? Did I mess up or something? I
thought today was pretty good."
"No, it was good."
It just brought back old wounds.
"You have real talent. If you put yourself out there, the world would
recognize you."
Lyn scoffed.
"Yeah, right, dumbass… That world? The one I loathe, the one I despise
to the point of nausea? That's exactly why I left."
"Lyn."
Kinsler approached her.
"You're Tyra Lyn, aren't you? It's me."
"Who are you?"
Of course, she didn't remember him.
"Ah, well, we were classmates. I was in the composition department at
Marius Academy of Arts. We even exchanged greetings after performances…"
Marius Academy was a painful name from her past, but Lyn was already far
beyond that world.
"Oh, wow. Haven't heard that name in a while. Nice to see you."
Her casual, carefree demeanor made something stir inside Kinsler—an
unshakable sense of inferiority.
"Yeah. I'm the director of the National Arts Institute now. Just taking
a break while working on a new score."
The words came out twisted, even without him realizing it.
But Lyn genuinely congratulated him.
"Wow, that's amazing. Really impressive."
"How about you? You shouldn't be performing in places like this. Why
don't you come with me? I can introduce you to people."
"Huh?"
As Lyn looked at him in confusion, Kinsler spoke triumphantly.
"I'm offering to help you. Surely, this is better than whatever it
is you're doing now. Don't you think?"
"Ohhh, so you heard about it?"
All Lyn wanted was to be free.
She had nothing but that freedom, so why did people always try to take
even that away?
"Sorry, but this was my last performance in this city. If you happen to
find me in another one, maybe I'll entertain your offer then."
"W-What do you mean? That's not what I was saying! I was genuinely
worried about you—"
"Haha!"
Lyn knew exactly what lay beneath those words of concern—the ridicule,
the judgment, the condescending relief that came from seeing someone supposedly beneath them.
She stuck out her tongue.
"The general public treating us like clowns is one thing. But you guys?
You're even worse."
At least those people knew they were mocking.
"You don't even have the guts to stake your life on
this."
"Bye-bye."
Leaving Kinsler red-faced, Lyn hooked her arm around Lanstein's and
walked away.
This was how they lived.
Even as people pointed at them with scorn, even as others wore masks of
pity to disguise their contempt—
This was their world.
Because they were free.
Far from everything, at the edge of the world, they lived like jesters
who laughed at it all.
