Chapter 754:
Doctrine of Evil (1)
Military Facility No. 48 was a site designated for special experiments
under the direct orders of President Igor.
Because of its importance, it was guarded by elite troops, and the
commander was a high-ranking officer personally appointed from the capital, Fascia.
Tap! Tap! Tap! Tap!
Every time soldiers patrolled the area—wearing studded boots and sabers
on their belts—civilians were stricken with fear.
"You! Come here!"
"M-Me?"
A man who had simply been walking down the road flinched and ran over
when he saw the killing intent in the soldier's eyes.
As soon as he arrived, the soldier's fist slammed into the man's
face.
"Ugh!"
Struck without even knowing the reason, the man collapsed to the ground,
too terrified to look up at them.
"Who the hell do you think you are? You see us patrolling so diligently,
and you don't even show praise to President Igor?"
There was no law like that in North Aymond's military code, but there
were over 300 clauses declaring "the soldier is the law."
The soldier must have been in a foul mood, and resisting here would only
mean being beheaded on the spot.
"I'm sorry! Long live President Igor!"
The man shot to his feet and raised both hands to the sky toward the
northern palace.
"…Get lost."
With the soldier's cold dismissal, the man dashed away, grateful to
still have his head.
His partner, watching from the side, asked,
"You're in a mood today. What's up?"
"Nothing. These people have no discipline anymore—ever since the Altar
opened. How's a country supposed to hold order like this?"
"Not getting your collections?"
The 80 soldiers stationed at Military Facility No. 48 collected "service
fees" from the 1,200 residents of the nearby city.
"They've been making excuses lately. Said their daughter's sick or
something. Kept delaying."
"Haha! Bad luck, huh? Well, when a kid's sick, a parent loses their
mind."
"It's not funny. I've gotta send money back home, and I spent too much
at the bar this month."
His partner winked.
"Want me to take care of it?"
"How? I already beat the crap out of them and still didn't get a cent.
If rumors get out in our unit, the commander's gonna be pissed."
"That's why experience matters, buddy."
His partner waved a finger and pointed toward the residential
zone.
"Sick or not—dying's worse, isn't it?"
Verdie, a twelve-year-old girl living in the Republic of North Aymond,
hated the world.
Why…
She didn't know exactly what happiness was, but she was sure she didn't
have it.
Even while delirious with fever, she had to cook for the soldiers who
had barged in that morning.
"That's why you should think it through. No one needs to lose a kid over
this, right?"
The soldiers were furious they hadn't been paid, and Verdie felt
miserable knowing her mother was being beaten because of her.
I can't be sick. I have to endure.
A thud came from the table being slammed, followed by her mother's
agonizing scream.
"Are you playing games right now?! You really want to see how far this
goes?!"
Verdie wanted to run out and give them all the money they had hidden—but
she knew nothing would change.
"Please! Not my daughter!"
"Get out of my way! And you dare call yourself a proud citizen of the
Republic of North Aymond?!"
Her hands chopped the carrots faster and faster until—slam!—the cutting
board hit the table.
It was a dagger she'd found in the mountains a month ago.
"…I want to kill them."
Lawslayer activated.
Why is it so quiet all of a sudden?
That thought lasted only a moment. The house soon filled with noise
again, and the soldier's partner entered.
"Hey! I'm starving here! How long is this gonna take?"
"Just a little longer! It'll be ready soon!"
Panicking, Verdie threw onions onto the board and started slicing
fast—too fast. Her fingertip got cut.
"Ow!"
She looked at the blood welling from her finger with a tearful face,
just as the partner kicked her.
"You stupid brat!"
He knocked Verdie to the floor and frowned as he looked at the
onion.
"You're sick, and you're getting blood on the food?"
The board had a bit of blood and flesh on it.
But what caught his eye was the dagger beside the board.
"Huh? What's this…"
It had a simple, plain shape—but it was immaculately clean, and when the
sunlight hit it, it shimmered with a faint purple glow. It was clearly no ordinary weapon.
"What's going on in here?"
The other soldier came into the kitchen.
"Look at this. She was hiding something like this. People who whine
always turn out the same."
Suddenly, the partner remembered something.
"What happened with the money?"
"It's handled. They're bringing it now. Should've done it sooner,
could've saved us the trouble."
"Heh, see? I told you to trust me. That's a pack of cigarettes for me,
yeah?"
Reaching into the basket, the partner grabbed a potato and tossed it
casually.
"No point cooking now. Let's just eat this and go."
Seeing the sprouted potato, the soldier flicked it back with his
wrist.
"Forget it. You eat it."
As the potato slapped into his hand, the partner got an idea.
"Okay, how about this? We play catch. Loser eats the potato. And owes me
a pack of smokes."
The soldier got into position.
"Fine by me. Throw it."
"Alright, here we go!"
Like a professional, he wound up and tossed the potato with smooth
motion—
Wait…?
The partner realized something.
Hold on. I'm right-handed, aren't I?
He'd caught the potato with his left hand.
"What was I holding in my right…?"
In that distracted moment, his hand—still gripping the dagger—swung
freely.
The full-force throw drove the blade directly into the soldier's
forehead.
"Guh!"
Verdie screamed and covered her face.
"Kyaaaaaaah!"
The partner checked the soldier—and saw his eyes half-bulging from their
sockets. He was dead on the spot.
"N-No! I didn't do this!"
He ran to Verdie.
"You saw it too, right?! We were just playing! Why'd I catch it with my
left hand?! I'm right-handed!"
Because, of course, his right hand had been holding the dagger.
"I-I don't know! I don't know anything!"
She really didn't—but her exaggerated cry came from a subconscious sense
of guilt.
"Verdie! What's going on?! Kyaaaaah!"
Her mother saw the body by the entrance, dropped the bundle she was
carrying, and screamed.
"H-He's dead!"
"You idiot! That's not what happened! It was an accident! Why would I
kill him?!"
As he shouted in denial, the partner suddenly had an idea. He yanked the
dagger out of the soldier's forehead.
"Y-Yeah! Let's say you killed him! That's it! Just say it was
you!"
He held the dagger out. Her mother recoiled in terror.
"Say it was you! I'll take care of everything! If this gets reported to
command, I'm done for!"
Threatening to frame her daughter for murder, he shouted—but the mother
could only shake her head in fear.
"Just listen to me!"
As he stepped forward in frustration, his foot landed on the fallen
soldier's hand.
A soft, squishy sensation tore through the sole of his boot, and his
heart sank.
"Ugh!"
His foot slipped, and he stumbled forward—just as something clicked in
his mind.
He's not going to die!
It wasn't reason—it was instinct. Something in him knew.
"Ghrrk!"
But then he looked into the dead soldier's eyes—and his body froze. He
twisted his ankle.
I-It wasn't me! I didn't kill him!
He tried to twist to the side, but his limbs had already locked up, and
his body crashed flat onto the floor.
"Guh!"
As he fell with his arms bent, the dagger buried itself deep into the
back of his neck.
"Grrk… Ggrrrrrk…"
Blood gurgled from his throat, his eyes bulging wide. Verdie's mother
clutched her head and wailed in anguish.
"Aaaaaah! Aaaaaaaaah!"
…This is Lawslayer.
A crime scene expert with sharp instincts might have noticed the
unnatural positions of the bodies—but in the end, no evidence would remain.
"Why is this happening to us? What are we supposed to do now…"
The situation was too absurd to be written off as coincidence—panic was
inevitable.
"I'm the one who killed them…"
And yet, strangely, with the threat gone, Verdie's heart was oddly
calm.
"What are you saying, sweetheart? These men died in an accident."
"No, Mom. To be exact…"
With a blank stare, Verdie walked up to the partner's corpse and pulled
the dagger from his hand.
"You… you…"
With trembling lips curled into a faint smile, Verdie turned to face her
mother.
"This knife granted my wish."
"You're insane! This is exactly when you need to stay calm!"
"Think about it. As long as we have this dagger, we can finally live
happily! No one will be able to hurt us again!"
There was no need to explain that it was an S-rank Object, one of the
highest tiers of weaponry.
"I'll get rid of them all. I'll punish everyone who ever hurt our
family! No—I'll destroy Igor himself!"
"You've really lost your mind…"
Her mother raised her voice, but Verdie was already entranced by
Lawslayer's magic.
"Knife, you can do it, right? You'll protect us to the end… here in this
hell…"
A man's voice came from outside the door.
"No. That's far enough."
Creaaak—though it had clearly been locked, the door opened effortlessly,
and a figure in a robe stepped inside.
"Wh-Who are you?"
She felt brief relief that it wasn't a soldier—but the calm look on his
face as he took in the two corpses made her heart pound in fear.
Death. Fatal injuries. No chance of survival.
Having confirmed this, Shirone took off his robe and stepped toward
Verdie.
"I'm here for the dagger. Hand it over. That's not something you should
be holding."
Verdie's eyes sharpened.
"Who even are you to say that? You're not from around here—get out
before I report you!"
Except for the port city of Dormica, foreigners weren't allowed in any
region of North Aymond.
"Two people are already dead. You should give it to me before the body
count increases."
"They deserved to die! They hurt my mom… and me…"
Tears welled up in Verdie's eyes.
"I'm not giving it to you! Without this, we'll be living in hell all
over again! No—I won't give it up!"
"That dagger is called Lawslayer."
Just hearing the name made her feel like it had been taken from
her.
"It's hard to explain, but it's similar to magic. It twists causality to
trigger murder. It's a vile weapon that's killed countless people for a very long time."
"Vile…?"
"There's a world of difference between wanting to kill someone… and
actually doing it. But Lawslayer doesn't see that difference."
Verdie quickly hid the dagger.
"I don't care if it's vile. This knife saved my mom. No one else ever
protected our family!"
Shirone nodded.
"I know you did your best. Lawslayer only activates when the desire is
truly desperate. You wanted to protect your family, didn't you?"
"Sniff… sniffle…"
Verdie began to sob.
"I didn't really mean to kill anyone… I just… I only wanted to protect
my mom…"
"I know. But Lawslayer isn't as kind as you are. That's why we need to
stop now. If it happens again, next time you will be the one who did it—not the dagger."
Shirone gently stroked her head.
"You can't protect your family with rage. If you keep relying on
something this twisted, you'll end up losing what really matters."
Verdie's face crumpled in fear as tears spilled from her eyes.
"Waaaaah! Mom! Mooooooom!"
"……"
Lifting the child's wrist, Shirone gently took hold of the blade and
carefully retrieved Lawslayer.
