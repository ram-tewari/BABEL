Chapter 76:
The Return of the Great Archmage (2)
Canis brought a teal robe. Though Arin was watching, Akein changed without hesitation.
Leaning on the table, Akein shuffled to a chair and finally caught his breath.
"Hah, I feel alive again."
"Are you alright, Master?"
Canis asked with concern. His dark circles contrasted sharply with his pale skin,
unmistakably marking him as a dark mage.
'What splendid eyes. That's why I took him as my disciple.'
In contrast, Akein wasn't fond of Arin. Her black hair and eyes indicated talent, but she
was weak-willed and lacked judgment.
Her pretty face showed no malice, and her large eyes betrayed every emotion.
Seeing the old man's naked body, her pupils trembled uncontrollably. Akein clicked his
tongue.
'Tch, tch. What use is she? Utterly worthless.'
If not for Canis's attachment, he wouldn't have brought such a fragile girl along.
Seven years ago, on that day.
Even with the life-support device, his mobility had deteriorated. Summoning his last
strength, he'd ventured into the capital, Vashka.
As the kingdom's largest city, its streets were wealthy and dazzling. But behind the
light lay the wretched lives of those cast aside by society.
The slum, Radum.
Reflecting the capital's extreme polarization, Radum's residents lived far more miserably
than beggars elsewhere.
Their only means of survival was scavenging through trash bins—competition so fierce that
murders over fish bones were commonplace.
There, Akein had found Canis and Arin.
In Radum, where daily life was war, ten-year-old Canis's survival tactic with Arin had
been to grow cruel.
Canis's eyes burned with hostility toward the world.
Akein liked that gaze. He took them to the dungeon and taught them dark magic.
'My eyes didn't deceive me. He's the real deal.'
Refusing Canis's help, Akein stood. Today, of all days, he didn't want to show
weakness—the day he'd escape wretched aging.
"Witness the greatness of dark magic."
Akein clutched the black crystal and closed his eyes. His hands shook weakly, but his
face was alight with excitement.
"Kuh—ah!"
With a grunt, the crystal softened, swirling around him like liquid before dispersing
like dark clouds and covering his body.
"Gaaaaah!"
Akein screamed like a monster. Absorbing 40 years of power at once would've driven an
ordinary person mad.
The concept of dark magic was absorption and assimilation.
Akein merged the dark power stored in the crystal with his own, reclaiming his former
strength.
"Uooooooh!"
The shadow at his feet flared like flames, then fragmented and scattered as if breaking
free.
Time passed.
Canis and Arin slowly opened their eyes. No trace of Akein's frailty remained.
His appearance hadn't rejuvenated, nor had his back straightened. But his complexion
glowed, and his eyes brimmed with vitality.
Feeling an aura of invincibility from his restored master, Canis trembled with awe.
'Is this Master's true form? I'd heard rumors, but this is astounding.'
Akein smiled, satisfied. This was his true power, as it had been before Alpheas wounded
him.
"Let's go. From today, Akein's history will be rewritten."
After waiting 40 years for this moment, Akein left the dungeon without savoring the surge
of power.
***
Akein arrived at Inferno, the western prison of Vashka. Housing criminals from class D to
B, it boasted impregnable security.
"Halt! Who goes there? This is a restricted area!"
The guards didn't lower their guard, even at the sight of an old man and children. They
drew their weapons and shouted warnings.
"Identify yourself! Failure to comply will result in arrest!"
"Greenhorns…"
Akein's shadow stretched, merging with the guards'. Before they could react, their eyes
rolled back, eyelids twitching, before they collapsed.
"Hmph, not even worth a snack. Canis, open the gate."
Canis took a key ring from a guard's belt and unlocked Inferno's main gate. Though Akein
could've bent the iron bars with ease, he seemed unwilling to escalate matters.
But Canis's assumption was quickly proven wrong. The moment they breached the gate, Akein
revealed his true nature.
"Intruders! Attack! Lethal force authorized!"
"Contact the palace! Request reinforcements! It's a mage!"
The guards charged with weapons drawn. But numbers meant nothing to a dark mage. Akein's
shadow extended like tendrils, merging with theirs.
"Guh—what is this?!"
"I can't move!"
The guards were paralyzed. Then, with a wave of Akein's hand, they turned on each other,
stabbing against their will.
"Agh! Are you insane? This is dangerous!"
"I don't know! It's not me!"
Arin paled. With her unique eyes, she perceived their unfiltered terror.
"Canis, Master never said we'd come here."
Canis was equally puzzled. But his trust in Akein remained unshaken.
"He must have a plan. Let's follow."
Akein strolled through the battlefield toward the building. When the shadows connecting
him to the guards snapped, the brawling men foamed at the mouth and collapsed.
This was the hallmark of dark magic—mind control.
Darkness merged with other darkness. By infiltrating another's shadow, one could control
their body and thoughts.
On paper, dark magic was overpowered.
Yet it remained marginalized because one flaw overshadowed all its strengths.
It was weak to light.
Under sunlight, darkness existed only as slivers of shadows. The stronger the light, the
more mental energy was needed to cast dark magic.
While dark mages reached their peak at night, even that advantage waned with the
popularization of photon theory, pioneered by the genius Kergos.
With dark magic students nearly extinct across the continent's magic academies, its
decline in magical society was evident.
But Canis knew Akein had perfectly overcome dark magic's weakness. And its ultimate
secret had been passed down to him.
'Yes, I must trust Master. He gave me everything.'
Breaking through the entrance, Akein dispatched more guards inside. He then entered Block
A-3, where prisoners were held. Sensing the commotion, inmates banged their meal trays against the bars.
"Hey! Don't care who you are—get us out!"
"Open this damn gate! We'll make it worth your while! Or we'll kill someone for you!"
Akein slowly raised his hands. The prison's grim atmosphere was the perfect stage for his
prowess.
"Authority of Darkness."
Shadows on the floor morphed into hands, rising three-dimensionally. Hundreds of hands
gripped the bars, wrenching them apart with metallic screeches. As terrified inmates shrank against the
walls, the bars were torn away one by one.
*Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang! Bang!*
The Authority of Darkness manipulated shadows to absorb surrounding energy, wielding them
like limbs. A single shadow could barely lift a pebble, but hundreds working in concert could rend iron bars
with monstrous strength.
Once the gates were open, the inmates staggered out, heavy iron balls chained to their
ankles.
"What, you a mage? Damn impressive."
"Thanks anyway. Name's Crown, the Night Cat. Folks in this hellhole know me."
Akein scowled at their wretched aura. Forty years ago, Inferno had been different. Once
filled with the worst of the worst, it had fallen into disrepute.
'How the mighty have fallen. Has life gotten so easy?'
In Akein's view, as civilization advanced, the quality of criminals declined. Con artists
proliferated, while true villains infiltrated society.
"Pathetic. Criminals introducing themselves? You think you're leaving just because the
bars are gone?"
"The hell, old man? Lost your marbles? We were trying to be nice!"
The inmates, emboldened by numbers, swaggered. Shared imprisonment didn't make them
allies.
"Listen well, fools. If you lack the strength to defeat me, stay in your cells. Defy me,
and you'll suffer broken bones."
The inmates burst into laughter.
"Pfft—ha! What's this geezer babbling about? You think we'll just go back after this?"
"Let's crush him! I'm sick of this damn prison!"
Drunk on freedom, they charged recklessly. But they soon realized their ankles were still
shackled.
Had they lost their minds? Or had they grown so accustomed to the chains they'd forgotten
them?
"Huh? What the—damn it!"
Panic set in. Akein watched their pitiful display and snorted.
To choose evil, one needed strength. Lacking that, at least intelligence. These were just
parasites, wasting food in prison.
"Authority of Darkness."
The hands that had torn the bars now wrapped around the inmates, dragging them into the
darkness. Like sinking into gelatin, their eyes, noses, and mouths vanished beneath the black veil.
Soon, the sound of snapping bones echoed.
"Mmph! Ugh!"
"Grrrrk!"
Muffled screams were all they could manage.
Hearing the ghastly sounds, Arin shuddered and turned to Canis.
"Canis, is this necessary for revenge?"
"Arin, denying Master is denying ourselves."
"But still…"
"Have you forgotten Radum? Worse things happened there. These prisoners are no different
from those who tormented us."
"But we promised. To forget everything from Radum. To be happy from now on."
"Nothing's over yet. Until Master's vengeance is fulfilled, we won't find happiness."
As the darkness faded, the inmates lay twisted on the ground, bones shattered.
Akein looked down at them disdainfully.
"Weaklings. Was this a waste of time? Or are the better ones in another block?"
Inferno held 320 prisoners. Perhaps only the dregs were housed here.
*Clank. Clank.*
Just then, the sound of chains came from a cell at the end of the hall. A wolf-like man
stepped forward, and Akein's eyes gleamed with interest.
Unlike the reckless fools, this one was different. Even better, he wore not just shackles
but handcuffs.
"One more remained, I see. Hiding your presence? Not bad."
The man surveyed the broken inmates and laughed hollowly.
"Heh, unbelievable. Do you know where this is? Inferno, the western prison. Planning
treason?"
"Treason. A delightful notion. Care to join?"
The man shrugged.
"If you're breaking me out, why not?"
"You heard me, didn't you? No freedom unless you defeat me."
"Tch, so strict."
The man scratched his head with his cuffed hands. As his eyes glinted between his arms,
he lunged without warning.
The weight of the iron ball didn't hinder his leap. His kick, aimed at Akein's face,
landed squarely—
*Crunch!*
The man grimaced as he landed. That sound when hitting a person?
"Tch, annoying magic."
Akein hadn't budged. The side of his face struck by the iron ball was covered in black
shadow. Dark Skin, a spell maximizing dark magic's absorption to nullify impact.
"Not bad. You pass."
Akein's Authority of Darkness summoned shadows that seized the man's shackles and cuffs.
"Don't move. Unless you want to lose limbs."
Before the man could retort, the shadows sharpened into blades and spun. The chains
snapped instantly, leaving his skin unharmed. Limitless shaping and precision were dark magic's pinnacle.
"Enjoy your freedom. Follow me. You have a role to play."
"What's this? I never agreed to join."
The man's smirk twitched.
He knew how strong Akein was. But being led like a cow would defeat the purpose of
escaping.
"Money moves you, doesn't it? Help me, and you'll be rich."
Mages were wealthy by default.
And the man would do anything for money.
"Heh, 'rich' better not be chump change."
Akein left the prison with his new follower in tow.
Thus, Inferno lost one prisoner that day.

Shirone's group rented an inn after organizing their research from the library. Two days
of discussion had outlined the photon cannon's principles.
"Why does mass exist?"
Without mass, nothing would change. And that wouldn't be so bad. Repeating the same day
wasn't the end of the world.
But mass existed from the beginning. As if the universe demanded novelty.
"Mass isn't weight. Weight is force exerted in gravity's direction. But space has no
direction. So what is mass? Cohesion? But that's not a perfect explanation either."
Neid asked,
"Then what do you think it is?"
The answer in Shirone's mind was clear, yet indistinct.
"Existence."
"Huh?"
"Proof of being in this space. That's mass."
Life was biology's code. Perhaps mass was the universe's true certificate of existence.
"Hmm. Sounds plausible. Like the Creator's will or breath."
Iruki snapped his fingers at Neid's words.
"The God Particle."
"What?"
"The God Particle. Not bad, right? Let's call the particle Shirone discovered the God
Particle."
"Oh, I like that! Shirone, what do you think?"
Shirone nodded repeatedly. It felt less like Iruki had coined the term and more like he'd
unearthed something preexisting—a perfect fit for what Shirone had sensed.
"I love it. And this is important."
"Right. A mage's power is tied to feeling. Using a name that resonates is best."
In that sense, the God Particle was an excellent choice.
With the discussion concluded, Iruki flopped onto the bed. A day of poring over books had
left him exhausted.
"Alright, let's wrap up the analysis and head back to school tomorrow. Classes resume the
day after, so mentally prepare yourselves. Don't tell me you've forgotten our promise already?"
Neid agreed.
"Of course. Moving up to Class Four this semester. From now on, we're studying like mad.
Worst case, Shirone might graduate before us."
It wasn't impossible.
What had started as a joke now made Neid and Iruki serious. Shirone might indeed graduate
first. The God Particle's discovery was that significant.
"Shirone, any chance you'd stay an extra year after graduating?"
"Hah! What are you talking about? Right now, I'm the most likely to break our promise. At
least you two are top-tier."
Shirone wasn't wrong, but his growth rate alone was cause for concern. A student who
hadn't known basic spells half a year ago was now considering graduation.
"Can't sleep. I'll study a bit before bed."
"Huh? Then me too!"
As Iruki grabbed a book, Neid rushed to claim the table.
Shirone was stunned. His usually carefree friends were burning with academic fervor. But
soon, he gazed at them warmly.
'I've met good friends.'
Shirone was the last to open his book.

The Royal Education Academic Council.
The Royal Academic Conference, hosted by the council, brought together magic and
swordsmanship schools, monastic orders, academic factions, alchemy foundations, and the elite 99 Artists to
discuss the kingdom's future.
Sade represented Alpheas Magic School. Days of debates on the pros and cons of rote
learning had nearly broken him.
'Ugh, damn it. Don't these people sleep? Let me go home.'
Drafted by lottery, he was miserable. A former delinquent, he had no interest in
education, voluntary or otherwise.
The only silver lining was the number of attractive female teachers. Especially the woman
across the round table—his ideal type.
Her wavy blue hair and playful demeanor, even in formal wear, were irresistible.
'She's adorable. Ozente Reina, was it?'
A member of the 99 Artists, the kingdom's premier artistic group, she attended as a
pianist under the mentorship of Melhen, the Melodious.
