Chapter 1:
Encountering Magic (1)
"Waah. Waah."
Vincent woke up with a frown. In the silent mountain where even birds were asleep, the
crying of a newborn baby could be heard.
"Waah. Waah."
The baby's sorrowful cries continued as he shook his disheveled head and rubbed his
sleepy eyes.
"God, what have I done so wrong?"
Coming down from the bed, Vincent looked back at his wife who was in deep sleep. He hoped
she was having a happy dream. If she heard this sound, she would surely have a hard time.
The Vincent couple, married for 7 years, had no children. They had even visited a doctor
spending a lot of money, but all they were told was that the cause was unknown.
"There's something called compatibility. It's just one of those things. Neither
Olina nor you seem to have any particular problem, so try often, many times. Haha!"
Vincent also laughed heartily at first. It was true that their relationship had improved
thanks to the doctor's prescription.
But as they entered their 5th year of marriage, he had to admit it.
He couldn't have children.
His wife, Olina, never expressed any resentment. But sometimes when she looked at other
people's children with a lonely expression, Vincent couldn't help but resent his own manhood.
"Which bastard is this, not just rubbing salt in my wounds!"
Due to the nature of his job as a hunter, he mostly lived in a house in the mountains.
This was necessary because he had to check traps regularly and needed a base camp in the mountains to track
wild animals for days.
No one would visit a hunter's house at this hour. Vincent, who couldn't rule out the
possibility of thieves, grabbed a single-edged axe and went out the door.
"Who's there! Who's making noise in the middle of the night!"
Vincent shouted loud enough to make the mountains echo. No one answered, and only his
shout returned as an echo.
It could be a merchant dealing with specialty products, but no matter how much he looked
around, not even a common torch was visible.
Vincent, becoming more tense, firmly gripped the axe handle. Then he slowly moved,
following the baby's cry.
The sound was coming from the stable.
It was highly likely to be thieves. He had heard from the slash-and-burn farmers' village
that robbers were rampant, targeting people living alone in the mountains.
"Damn you! I'll cut you to pieces!"
In the worst case, blood would have to be spilled.
With such resolution, Vincent flung open the stable door. At the same time, he surveyed
the inside with a hunter's excellent senses.
Purr.
Hearing the peaceful snorting of horses, he felt somewhat calmed. This was because
animals do not lie.
The stable, where two horses were sleeping, was small and cozy. As such, there was
nowhere to hide. But surprisingly, there was no sign that anyone had entered.
"Then how could the crying sound...?"
Vincent carefully moved his steps and examined the area covered with straw. A newborn
baby, about two months old, was crying.
He hid the axe behind his back, feeling an inexplicable sense of holiness. Then, thinking
even this was irreverent, he threw it to the corner and crawled toward the baby.
"Waah. Waah."
When he opened the swaddling cloth with his rough hands, there was a baby as beautiful as
the moon. An innocent baby who knew nothing and had just encountered the air of the world.
Vincent's pupils trembled. Then, as if struck by lightning, he shook his body and, unable
to control his emotions, darted out of the stable.
"Who's there! Who's playing tricks! Abandoning a child, you rotten bastard! Come out
now!"
The echo resounded as if several people were speaking.
"Come out! Are you really not coming out? How could you abandon a child! You're
truly a bad person! You know that?"
He urged once more, but there was no answer.
"You really abandoned it! No more chances! Just try appearing before me! I'll make
your face into rice cake!"
Vincent shouted with the loudest voice of his life. He didn't want to leave any regrets
when reminiscing about today in the distant future.
Vincent entered the stable again. The baby had fallen asleep, exhausted from crying. With
trembling hands, he embraced the baby and gently placed his ear against the small chest.
The heart was beating.
"Honey, what's going on?"
His wife, Olina, entered the stable, gasping for breath. Vincent's voice had been loud
enough to completely wake her from sleep.
Vincent, at a loss for words, stared blankly at his wife before showing her the baby
sleeping in his arms.
"What's that baby?"
"It seems... it's our child."
***
In the early summer, when blue was rampant.
Vincent hurried his steps, humming a tune while following the path of cold flowing
stream. A large roe deer was hanging over his shoulder, but the movements of his large body were only light.
As home drew near, his steps quickened. The faces of his precious family were already
before his eyes.
"Shirone, Dad's home!"
"Dad!"
A 12-year-old boy ran to the entrance with a broad smile. Unlike Vincent, who was like a
rugged rock, Shirone had grown into a beautiful boy, as if specially cherished by God.
With his golden hair flowing like golden thread, blue eyes, and a straight nose bridge
even though not fully grown, he looked like a doll. Every time he saw his son like this, Vincent couldn't
help but feel proud and stand tall.
Embracing Shirone, he buried his face in his son's nape and took a deep breath. His son's
scent completely washed away the fatigue of the tiresome mountain trek.
"So, my son. My treasure. Have you been well?"
"Yes. I helped Mom with cooking and read many books."
Cooking and books.
Feeling the dissonance between these two words, Vincent was perplexed. But he didn't show
it and burst into hearty laughter.
"Do you like books that much?"
"No, well... there's not much else to do."
Seeing his son hiding his emotions as if he had done something wrong, Vincent felt sorry
and guilty.
In fact, he knew. That this miraculous child given by heaven was extraordinarily
intelligent.
Though Olina knew a little about writing, she was still a mountain hunter's wife.
Nevertheless, Shirone had stumbled through learning letters with just what he had been taught, and now he
had reached a level where he could read books on his own.
That's why it hurt even more.
A hunter's child couldn't study.
A herbalist's child becomes a herbalist, a hunter's child becomes a hunter—that was the
only way to make a living.
No matter how humble a profession, there are secrets and knacks that cannot be passed on
by word alone. The only thing that poor Vincent could pass on to his son was hunting skills.
But Vincent couldn't bring himself to say it. Twelve was too young to learn about the
unfairness of the world.
"No, you did well. Whatever you do, you need to learn to succeed. This time when Dad
goes to the city, I'll buy you a book."
"It's okay. You've already bought me many. And honestly, they didn't have much
content."
Vincent laughed at his son's bluff. Practical books that could make money were too
expensive to even consider, and he had bought discarded books from nobles at bargain prices from antique
shops. As far as he knew, they weren't books at a level that a child could digest.
Vincent's heart was touched yet torn by his son's mindset, worrying about his parents'
living first.
"Alright! By the way, how about it? Want to go chop wood with Dad? Learning is
important, but a man needs physical strength. Today, I'll teach you how to chop wood."
"Wow! So you're giving me an axe too?"
"Haha! Of course! Today, let's chop down all the trees in the mountain with
Dad!"
Vincent gave Shirone an axe as if he had been waiting for this moment. Though it was
expensive compared to their living, it was much more useful than books. An axe could help earn money.
Honestly, he wanted his son to become a mountain hunter. But Shirone was small for his
age and didn't seem capable of doing such hard work. So he thought of building his strength from now on.
'His face has noble features and his mind is exceptionally good. Could he have been a
noble's child?'
Vincent hastily erased this doubt from his mind. Every time he had such thoughts, he felt
overwhelmed as if receiving an undeserved gift, only to be followed by guilt.
'Shirone is my child. Not a child taken from a stable, but my child with my blood.'
With such determination, Vincent took Shirone's hand and left the house.
His logging area was about 1 kilometer away from the hut.
Because there were many mountain hunters nearby, fights could break out if one worked
outside the designated area. Sometimes it even led to knife fights.
"Now. I'll demonstrate, so watch carefully and follow."
Vincent spit on his palm and then started chopping the tree with a refreshing motion. Not
long after the thudding sound started, the straight tree fell with a crackling noise.
The number of axe strokes was a measure of a lumberjack's skill. Vincent was not a
lumberjack, so he toppled it with 10 strokes, but even that was an impressive skill.
"This way, you chop the same spot several times, and when the tree leans, it
collapses unable to bear its weight. Can you do it?"
"Yes, I'll try."
Vincent chose a tree that Shirone could chop. Standing in front of the tree, Shirone spit
on his hands just as his father had done. Though he had only seen it once, his positioning, posture, and
even the habit of rubbing his palms were identical to his father's.
Vincent smiled happily, thinking indeed, he was smart.
But when he actually tried to swing the axe, his posture was somehow flimsy.
It's not something that can be done with just the head. Strength and physical condition
must support it. The axe was quite heavy, and strong muscles were essential to swing it enough to cut
through the wind.
The reason Vincent had brought Shirone to chop wood was that his build was smaller
compared to his peers.
If he couldn't become a scholar, he should at least build strength. No woman would want
to marry a man who couldn't earn money.
"Heek! Eek!"
Shirone gritted his teeth and swung the axe. But each swing hit a different spot.
Unable to watch any longer, Vincent gave him some tips.
"Don't use all your strength. Instead of that, reduce your power a bit but increase
your accuracy."
Shirone quickly absorbed Vincent's advice. But this time, his strength was too weak,
showing no signs of breaking the tree.
Was my son always this weak?
Vincent became a bit downcast.
"Phew, this is hard."
"It's okay, Shirone. No, I'm sorry. To be honest, I know that this kind of work
doesn't suit you. But the child of a hunter, inevitably..."
Vincent was at a loss for words due to a surge of emotion. How would a parent feel having
to convey the harsh reality to their child?
"You're a really intelligent child. More than Baron, the herbalist's son, more than
Stella, the fruit seller's daughter. You don't need to feel inferior because you lack strength. My greed
is..."
Tears welled up in Vincent's eyes due to his sorrowful feelings. But Shirone paid no
attention to that and pulled his father's collar, asking:
"More importantly, Dad, how can I chop the tree well?"
Vincent felt a bit embarrassed.
But the embarrassment was brief, and when Shirone showed interest in wood chopping, a
sense of elation swept over him.
"Do you really want to learn this?"
"Yes, teach me. It's fun."
His son's words gave him courage again. Vincent, reinvigorated, took Shirone and examined
the notch in the tree closely.
"Now, look at this notch. Strength will come naturally when you grow up. But in
fact, this isn't a job that requires a lot of strength. What's important is technique. I told you earlier to
chop the same spot, but if you twist the angle just a little, it will receive much more force."
"Ah, I see."
Vincent squinted his eyes and stared intently at the notch Shirone had made. It was quite
surprising. He had hit the exact same spot each time, which was hard to believe for a beginner.
At this rate, it would actually be difficult to fell the tree unless one had exceptional
strength.
