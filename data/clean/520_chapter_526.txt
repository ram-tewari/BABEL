Chapter 526:
Fermi didn't seem particularly surprised.
Thinking he was better than Enrique, Miro entered the room with Arius in
tow.
"Disappointing. It's summer break and in your prime, yet here you are
holed up doing dishes. I was hoping to catch you doing something weird so I could tease you."
"Doing something weird over break wouldn't be very productive. Why are
you here?"
Miro, now seated on the sofa, shifted the topic.
"You really remember me? I haven't seen you since you were three. I'd
call out, 'Goldie! Goldie!' and you'd giggle and run over."
"I remember everything since my brain started functioning."
"You definitely take after your mom. Your dad can't even remember what
he did yesterday. Oh, so… the baldness might be…"
"Haa…"
Fermi was starting to get annoyed.
"Just get to the point. Why are you here?"
"You heard I came back from Heaven, right?"
"Nope."
Assuming that might be true, Miro pulled back the robe covering
Shirone.
"Tada. What do you think? Does this catch your interest?"
Fermi glanced at the unconscious Shirone for a moment, then looked back
at Miro.
"Familiar face."
During the final year ranking evaluations, Shirone had dropped out, and
Fermi hadn't heard anything since.
"Shirone was in Heaven too. Fought for the fate of the world. Don't you
want to know what happened?"
Fermi leaned back in the sofa, clearly uninterested.
"Not really."
"Come on, listen. In Heaven, there was this bizarre creature called Anke
Ra—"
"Whatever."
Fermi cut her off.
"Isn't that the kind of thing the Adrias family should be
handling?"
Miro gave a rueful smile and bit her lip.
"Whether the world ends or some monster rules another dimension—it
doesn't matter to me. Same goes for Shirone. If he comes back to school, I'll crush him. If he doesn't,
so be it."
"Really? Then why'd you enroll in Alpheas Magic Academy?"
"What are you getting at?"
There was a flash of madness in Fermi's eyes.
"It has nothing to do with you. The Adrias family is nothing but a relic
of a failed legacy."
Even before he could speak properly, the Ardino family had already
suffered through much. Nineteen years later, Fermi had become a Regulation Exception user.
"I'm sorry for the damage I caused. I mean it. But I had no
choice."
"Don't fool yourself. I enrolled in Alpheas for my own goals. There's a
lot of money to be made there."
Miro smiled again.
"Then how about you use that drive to achieve a new goal?"
Like a cat retracting its claws, the madness in Fermi's gaze vanished
instantly.
Flipping at the prospect of money—that was classic Ardino. Still, Miro
couldn't help but feel he was trying hard to mask something.
"I'm not the type to refuse a deal. But since the offer's from my aunt,
I feel like it needs some conditions."
"Name them."
Fermi pointed at the floor.
"If you kneel before me and apologize, I might consider it. I am still a
member of the Ardino family, after all—I can't just make deals lightly, can I?"
"That's easy."
Miro was completely sincere.
"Kneeling means nothing to me. If you carry the blood of Adrias,
changing your mind is like flipping your hand. But let me ask you something."
"What?"
"Do you really think there's any difference, for me, between kneeling
before you and killing you right here and now?"
"…"
There wouldn't be.
The Miro he knew—Adrias Miro—wasn't bound by anything.
"Haha!"
Fermi suddenly burst into a full smile, like none of that had just
happened.
"Kidding. Would I really ask my aunt to do something like that? But I
will expect proper compensation for the deal."
"That's fine. I happen to have an incredibly powerful sponsor backing
me."
"Now that's good to hear. Alright, let's get started. What do you need
from me?"
Miro relayed everything she had told Uorin, including Uorin's
conclusions.
"Hmm… extracting the collateral, huh?"
Sharp as ever, Fermi understood immediately.
He also realized the scale of this deal—especially with names like Miro
and Teraje involved.
"Well, now I'm feeling a bit motivated."
That was good. Beyond Fermi's ability, what Miro truly needed was the
vast magical knowledge the Ardino family possessed.
"Can you extract Shirone's magic?"
Instead of answering, Fermi raised the hand resting on the
armrest.
Regulation Exception – Depreciation Trade.
With a pleasant sound, colorful and patterned chips scattered below his
palm.
As the chips stacked neatly into a tower, Fermi flicked the top chip
into the air.
"Fancy. What spell is that?"
Catching the falling chip, Fermi answered,
"Summoning spell: Epoxy Tosca."
"Oho."
Miro caught the chip Fermi tossed her.
It was patterned in mottled two-tone colors, with an insect resembling a
spider drawn in the center decal.
"It's a large bug that lives around Lake Tosca. It latches onto human
spinal cords, secretes a hallucinogen, and drinks blood. Under the hallucination, the victim speaks only
truth. If removed by force, it releases acid and self-destructs—completely dissolving the body within 48
hours."
"Perfect for crime."
"Exactly. I bought it through a palace contact. Premium spell, cost a
fortune."
"Can you extract any spell?"
As Miro tossed the chip, Fermi caught it again.
"Basically, yes. Regulation Exception magic that steals or copies
others' spells usually requires complicated conditions or dangerous prices during equivalent exchange.
But for me, those aren't necessary."
"Because you use the equivalent exchange itself as a tool."
Fermi grinned like a fool.
"Exactly. I'm just the intermediary that completes the Regulation
Exception. When magic is traded, the will of both seller and buyer must be reflected."
Since both parties had already agreed, there was no special cost or
condition for Fermi.
"The medium balancing both sides is money, I assume. Alright. Then how
do you determine the price?"
Fermi extended his left hand.
Regulation Exception – Depreciation Trade Contract.
A single sheet of paper materialized into his hand.
"The price is based on the broker's rational judgment according to the
situation. Of course, lying isn't possible. If an irrational price is set—"
"The Regulation Exception won't activate."
"Correct. The advantage of this spell is that the broker doesn't have to
meet any separate conditions. Ultimately, the decision lies with the parties involved. But there's one
issue."
"You mean with the traders?"
"Yes. Things like 'regret' or 'changing one's mind.' Permanently
transferring magic requires a level of risk that goes beyond just magical value."
"So that's why you apply depreciation."
"Exactly. After much thought, I concluded that creating the possibility
of 'contract reversal' was necessary. If you can undo the contract, it eases customer anxiety and
reinforces the Regulation Exception."
Fermi placed the trade contract on the table.
"If the value of a magic A is 100 gold for a one-month rental, then with
depreciation, the second month would be 60 gold, and the third month 30 gold. It can be terminated,
transferred, or extended at any time."
A smile played on Miro's lips as she looked over the contract.
With depreciation in play, any deal could be made reasonable.
"Alright. Once the value's been set, what's next?"
"We determine the commission. I set the fee on all funds exchanged
between the clients—anywhere from 1% to 99%."
"Pfft..."
Miro lowered her head, laughing quietly.
The value of magic couldn't be falsified, but the commission—that, Fermi
could assign however he pleased.
Which, of course, made perfect sense.
No one creates a Regulation Exception like this out of the kindness of
their heart.
And by arbitrarily setting the fee, he could maneuver both seller and
buyer into positions of his choosing.
"I knew it. Honestly, if even this much wasn't in place, I would've
given up on the Ardino family. So, let's hear it—what do you think the Valhalla Action is worth?"
Fermi leaned on his elbow, falling into thought.
He couldn't lie—he had to state a number that he genuinely believed
in.
Deciding there was no further need for calculation, Fermi slowly opened
his mouth.
"100 billion gold."
Arius swallowed hard.
He recalled when he'd tried to sell Ataraxia to Zion—40 billion gold,
plus a position in Teraje's faction.
Sure, that position was worth more than the gold itself, and Fermi's
valuation was based on "context," but even so—100 billion was a number Arius couldn't have even
imagined.
Well… if a pro says so, I guess it is what it is…
Arius, now certain he should never go into business, felt dwarfed by the
scale of Fermi's thinking.
"We'll base it on a three-year contract. 100 billion gold in the first
year, 60 billion in the second, and 30 billion in the third. If we're looking at a total deal size of
around 200 billion gold, we should be able to extract the Valhalla Action."
Miro wasn't particularly surprised.
She'd already guessed Teraje had offered a blank check—and it wasn't
coming out of her own pocket anyway.
"Good. And I'm guessing the commission will be… 99 percent?"
Fermi simply shrugged, as if there was no need to even ask.
"Let's draw up the contract, then. Once both the seller and buyer sign,
the deal is complete."
"I can go get Teraje's signature, but Shirone's in a moratorium—he can't
sign. Just putting a pen in his hand and scribbling doesn't count, does it?"
"Of course not."
"So how do we get his signature?"
Fermi blinked.
"Why are you asking me that? That's your problem to solve."
"Goldie."
Miro gave him a sweet smile.
"You really think I came all the way here because I couldn't find
someone who can extract magic? A deal this big—you're seeing it through to the end. If you're such a
genius broker, you should have a way, even in this situation."
Despite Miro's push, Fermi still looked bored.
"If you don't know, how am I supposed to?"
"No—you do know."
"What makes you think that?"
"Because even after I told you Shirone was in moratorium, you went ahead
and explained the depreciation trade. That means you know there's a way."
"Don't jump to conclusions. That kind of talk doesn't pressure a
merchant at all."
"Really? Then let me speak in a merchant's language."
Leaning back in her chair, Miro crossed her arms and legs.
"At first, I wondered why you created a magic like this. If it was just
to make money, Ardino has more than enough financial muscle."
"There you go again, assuming."
"Just hear me out. What you really want is access to top-tier magic—the
kind with astronomical value. That's your goal, right? Which means your reason for creating this magic
becomes obvious."
Fermi finally turned to look at her.
"There's one thing missing from your explanation. The condition you have
to fulfill in order to arbitrarily set the commission."
Miro smiled like someone who already knew she'd won.
"When a broker buys chips to use for themselves, the cost must be paid
entirely with commission income accrued up to that point."
'You've gotta be kidding me…!'
A string of curses raced through Fermi's head.
