Chapter 73:
Dream On (2)
About ten children sat at the table as Rumina's mother generously served them boiled
meat.
Shirone felt awkward. Preparing this much food must've cost a fair amount. Paying for it
wasn't an issue, but that would've been strange in itself.
As if reading his mind, Altol said, "Don't worry. We can sell the monster hides for
money. We'll start tomorrow, with your permission, of course."
"Huh? Oh, of course. And the monsters are called Wulke. Their claws and fangs should
fetch a good price."
Shirone had expected as much. Knowing other parts were valuable meant even greater
profits.
But more than that, he was glad to learn the monsters' name.
In this world, knowledge was also money.
Selling them without even knowing their name would've been a disaster. They might've lost
more than the effort spent skinning them.
"I see. Thanks. Without you, our village would've had to relocate."
For generations, they'd tilled the fields and sold forest goods. Losing their home
would've been devastating. In that sense, Shirone was the hero who'd saved the village.
"It's fine. This place is important to me too."
Shirone meant it. Even though the Wulke meat would spoil and become unsellable, the
children had stayed instead of heading back to the mountains.
They weren't wealthy. Life was hard, and they blushed more often than nobles. But they
knew how to live together, and he liked that.
Martin, chewing on meat, said, "Sorry your vacation turned out like this. Wanna come up
the mountain tomorrow? We'll sell the hides and claws and have fun with the money."
The slash-and-burn kids never missed a chance to play, but Shirone regretfully declined.
He wanted to return and analyze his newfound insights as soon as possible.
"No, I've rested enough. Thanks to you, I'm not tired anymore. I need to study hard now.
I'll head back to school tomorrow morning."
***
After dinner, the children returned to their homes. Though the moon was out, they still
had work to do—repairing gear and checking carts.
Shirone climbed the hill and looked down at the slash-and-burn village. The children
carried torches, preparing for the next day's work while joking around. Their cheerful banter brought a soft
smile to his lips.
"Shirone, there you are."
"Huh? Altol, how'd you find me?"
"Just had a feeling. What's on your mind? Didn't even notice me coming."
"Old memories. I used to run around here with my dad. Played tag in that field."
"Yeah. But you spent way more time reading alone. Probably because of us."
"No, I just loved books…"
Altol laughed warmly. "No need for that. It's obvious. The kids have always admired you.
I was jealous sometimes. Looking back, I owe you more apologies than I can count."
"No, I was the odd one. Maybe I was scared of meeting people. It was a confusing time."
In the slash-and-burn village, Shirone was the pride of his parents, unlike the other
kids who caused trouble. But in truth, after discovering magic at twelve, his life had been endless turmoil.
Altol nodded knowingly. Seeing Shirone's magic today had made things clear.
The boy they'd thought was a bookish loner had been fighting harder than anyone.
"Sorry, Shirone."
"It's fine."
"No, not that. At the drinking party. I overstepped. Everyone has their limits. I was
wrong. Don't hold it against me."
Shirone recalled Altol's blunt words from last night. It hadn't hurt. It was just the
burden he'd chosen by pursuing magic.
"I never thought about it. Honestly, I'd never realized how blessed I am. How lucky."
"No, Shirone. You worked hard. That's why people help you."
"But it's the truth. What you said is my reality, something I'll carry forever."
Altol smiled bitterly. No one in the slash-and-burn village could criticize themselves
like that.
"Mages are something else."
Their way of thinking was incomprehensible. But perhaps that was why the world revered
them.
Shirone and Altol gazed at the village. Though neither spoke, their eyes were fixed on
the same sight.
***
The next morning.
The adults had started their day at dawn, leaving only the children behind.
Shirone ate breakfast at Rumina's house and prepared to leave.
The children saw him off, though they were laden with tools for dismantling the Wulke.
In their relentless lives, Shirone realized just how weighty his own path was.
"Take care, Shirone. Study hard. Become a great mage."
Altol offered a handshake. The others crowded around, saying their goodbyes.
"Shirone, you'll visit again, right?"
"Tell us all about magic school next time."
"Haha! Sure. I'll come back after the term ends. You all take care."
After bidding farewell, Shirone turned to Rumina. Amid the cheerful atmosphere, only she
looked somber.
Shirone had an inkling of her feelings. But what could he do? Matters of the heart
weren't decided by logic.
"Rumina, thanks for all the delicious food. You'll cook for me again, right?"
Rumina finally smiled. It was a resigned smile. She knew wanting more would be greedy.
"Of course! So visit often. If you act all high and mighty as a mage, I'll beat you up."
"Yeah. I'll definitely come back."
As Shirone descended the hill, he suddenly turned back. Shouting to the children still
watching, he said, "Stay safe! Stay healthy!"
When Shirone turned and walked away, Rumina finally hung her head. More painful than his
departure was the realization that she could no longer love him.
"Hey, you okay?"
Altol placed a hand on her shoulder. Rumina couldn't hold back her tears. Altol's heart
ached. He'd suspected her childhood crush, but hadn't realized how deep it ran.
"Tch, if you're this upset, you should've said something. Want me to drag him back for
you?"
"No, it's fine. Shirone isn't really leaving."
Rumina wiped her tears with both hands and looked up. A bright smile had returned to her
face.
"Shirone is… chasing his dream."
Rumina prayed to the gods of the mountain and forest.
May warm light guide his path.
***
On his way back to school, Shirone pondered the questions the slash-and-burn village had
raised.
Just how far could he go?
Becoming a guest of the Ozent family or studying with magic school students didn't mean
he'd achieved anything.
The advice to learn from failure and persevere didn't apply to a commoner like him.
He had to explode his talent relentlessly. He couldn't afford to hesitate.
Thanks to the Ozent family's support, he attended school. His parents' sacrifices let him
study advanced magic.
Was this the right path?
If he failed, could he accept it and live as before?
Arriving at school, Shirone headed to the research club. Feeling unsettled, he didn't
want to return to the dorm.
'Are the others having fun? I hope they didn't lose much…'
By now, Iruki and Nade were probably betting wildly at the casino. He just hoped they
hadn't wagered the house deeds.
Passing through the maze of Istas, Shirone stopped at the warehouse door. Voices leaked
through the gap.
"Huh?"
Inside, Iruki and Nade were arguing on the sofa. The debate was so heated that Iruki was
unusually worked up.
"You idiot! A perpetual motion machine is impossible!"
"Why are you so closed-minded? I'm not talking about infinite energy but a second-kind
perpetual motion machine!"
"That violates the laws of entropy!"
"Then what's this? What do you call this?!"
Nade pointed at a wind-up cart on the table. It was more of a wheeled metal box than a
cart.
"Huh? Hey…"
Nade turned to Shirone, then cut to the chase, shaking the cart in his face.
"Shirone! Tell him. I made this. You wind it up like this, then put it down…"
The cart moved in circular motions, yet the winding didn't loosen.
Iruki glared, pointing accusingly. "Hey, Shirone! Don't fall for it. It's a total scam!"
"What scam? You were waiting to see when it'd stop before you started this!"
"Idiot! If it could last 1-2 hours, anyone could make one! Yours is just highly
efficient! I tried winding it—it's ridiculously tight!"
"So what? It's a miniaturized version! A full-scale one would be 100 times more
efficient!"
"How big are you planning to make it? Sky-sized? Feeding a horse would be more
efficient!"
"I just need to show it's possible! Technology trends toward miniaturization over time!"
"Without theory, technology collapses!"
"Who cares about theory? Just make it and use it!"
The mathematician and the engineer both had points. Shirone blinked, listening, then
examined the cart.
Watching them argue over a useless cart made his earlier worries vanish.
'Ah… I see.'
Someday, that cart would earn a proper name through scholars' research. And someone would
use it to create new knowledge.
How arrogant to think only he could do something. He hadn't come this far because he was
special.
If his father hadn't bought him books, if he hadn't joined the Ozent family, if there'd
been no teleportation test, if he hadn't met Iruki and Nade…
Shirone wouldn't exist.
A chain of tiny, improbable coincidences had led him here.
'Thank you. For letting me be here.'
Lost in emotion, Shirone quickly smiled and asked his friends, "So, how was the casino?"
Nade, forehead-to-forehead with Iruki, turned with a grumpy look. "How? We lost
everything in one day and came back. One more bet, and we'd have hit the jackpot."
"I told you it'd be Spades. A 57% chance."
"Funny! I listened to you and lost a ton! 57% my foot! How's that different from
gambling?"
