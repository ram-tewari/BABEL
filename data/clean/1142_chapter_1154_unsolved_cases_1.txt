Chapter 1154:
Unsolved cases (1)
Melkidu, Dorian Village.
Shirone's group entered the tavern and ordered a simple meal and some
beer from the bartender.
The man who introduced himself as a detective was named Curtis.
He was from the Iron Kingdom and had been in Melkidu for two years
now.
Ten minutes later, their food arrived.
"How do we pay?"
Shirone asked, watching as Curtis gulped down his beer and wiped his
chin.
"Ahh… You don't need to. Money doesn't exist here. This is a
sanctuary for murderers."
"Then how does it work?"
"You just leave. As long as you have Crime Points, criminal acts go
unnoticed by people. A single beer costs 2P as a crime."
"Ah, I see."
Shirone roughly understood the system.
"Stabbing someone with a knife costs 5P, so it's quite expensive.
Of course, as you complete cycles, Crime Points accumulate, so eventually, it won't feel like a
burden."
Iruki asked,
"Does 'cycle' mean that Melkidu is structured like an
orbit?"
"That's right. More precisely, it has two tracks—an outer track and
an inner track. The outer track consists of 468 blocks, where most of the missions take place. However,
upon reaching certain blocks, you can enter the inner track."
"And what's inside the inner track?"
"It consists of 197 blocks, where you can enhance the Crime Dice or
purchase special items from shops. The key feature is that you can only complete a single loop around
the inner track. Once you've traveled through all its blocks, you are forcibly sent back to the outer
track. However, there's one exception—if you enter Melkidu itself."
Eden asked,
"Isn't this place already Melkidu?"
"Of course, but the only place that truly embodies the essence of
Melkidu is the Core. Every player gathers Crime Points in the outer track to enter the inner track, and
while looping through the inner track, their goal is to reach the Core. If they fail to reach the Core,
they are forced back into the outer track, repeating this insane cycle of murder over and over
again."
"Why do murderers want to reach the Core? To escape
pursuit?"
Curtis sighed.
"… It's beyond that."
He gripped his beer mug, lost in thought for a moment, before downing
the rest of his drink.
"I've never been to the Core myself, so I can't say for sure. But
I've heard the rumors. That's why I've been running in circles on this track for two years."
His gaze darkened.
"She was twenty-seven—my daughter, Edrena. She loved to travel.
That day, she was excited about going to the beach with her friends. It was just another normal day. I
remember her packing her bags, putting on makeup, kissing her mother and me on the cheek before
leaving."
"So it was his daughter…"
"She was supposed to go with her friends. They were close—Nia,
Belita, Daisy. They planned to meet at Sif Plaza before heading out. But then…"
"She disappeared."
Shirone recalled reading about it in the international news section back
when he was studying at Tormia's Magic Academy.
"Yeah. Broad daylight. Nia and Belita lived in the city, while
Daisy lived uptown. According to a witness, my daughter was seen at Sif Plaza, looking extremely
anxious. Another said she was reading something on the plaza's bulletin board. But that's all the
information we ever got. After that, my daughter vanished without a trace."
"Could it have been the work of someone she knew?"
"What do you take me for? I investigated everything I could. It was
useless. What's even more shocking is that her friends also disappeared around the same time. The plaza
was two kilometers away from where they lived, yet they all vanished almost simultaneously."
Curtis lit a cigarette.
"I searched for three years. The underworld, human trafficking
rings, magic, occult practices—I looked into everything. Then, I came across rumors about Melkidu.
That's what led me here."
"Does that mean you believe you can find your daughter in
Melkidu?"
"Yes."
He exhaled smoke with a heavy sigh.
"The Core of Melkidu is a place where all the world's crimes are
recorded. It's the archive of unsolved cases. If I can clear this murder game, I can reach
it."
"Wait… what do you mean?"
"Why do you think this place is called a 'sanctuary for murderers'?
If a murderer clears this game, the crimes they committed in reality become perfect crimes—completely
unsolvable. In other words, they become permanent cold cases."
It sounded completely irrational.
"How could that even happen? I mean, real unsolved cases exist, but
what about witnesses and evidence…? Ah."
A thought crossed Shirone's mind.
"Crime Points."
Crimes committed using Crime Points in Melkidu were not perceived by
others.
"Exactly. I believe this place operates under a specific system. As
players accumulate points by running laps around the tracks, those points grant them a special power
that affects reality. Once a murderer reaches the Core, they can manipulate their records and return to
the real world. In other words, they can commit more crimes with total immunity."
Iruki muttered,
"It's similar to Havitz's 'Vanishing.' The hidden codes probably
share the same principles. Just like how Drimo allows objects to be extracted into reality, the Crime
Points accumulated here seem to have a direct impact on the real world."
Curtis extinguished his cigarette.
"No matter what, I'm going to find my daughter. I'll gather enough
Crime Points to enter the Core. It won't be easy. There are only two ways in—either collect an enormous
amount of points or be extremely lucky. There's also a rumored method involving a special mission in the
inner track that lets you bypass the point requirement, but information is so scarce that it's mostly
regarded as a myth."
"The Devil's Bible."
Shirone thought of Gyorgi, whose abilities had allowed the members of
the Catacombs to escape.
As he processed everything Curtis had told them, the tavern door
suddenly swung open.
"Candles for sale! Candles for sale!"
"Huh?"
Nade's eyes widened.
"That woman…?"
It was the same woman who had been stabbed in the throat and killed on
the street just thirty minutes ago.
Curtis turned around.
"Most non-player characters in Melkidu resurrect. Of course, there
are some event-specific NPCs who don't, and the resurrection time varies depending on the
mission."
"Hmm."
Realizing they weren't actual humans made Shirone's group feel slightly
relieved.
"It's still unsettling, though. Even if they're fake, they react
and feel exactly like real people. At first, I only targeted criminals while clearing missions. This
place really does mimic a real society. But that method is inefficient. Eventually, I became as
desensitized as the other killers."
Perhaps that was the true nature of Melkidu.
"There are plenty of other methods. You could set buildings on fire
or use a repeating crossbow to massacre a crowd in the streets. But crimes like that consume a lot of
Crime Points. That killer earlier? He stabbed a passerby with a knife he bought from the weapon shop.
That's because Dorian's C-rank mission doesn't offer much of a reward. No matter how well you roll the
dice, it's hard to get more than 200P. That's why he chose the most efficient, low-cost
method."
Shirone understood.
"So he didn't fight us because he wanted to conserve his
points."
"Exactly. The rule that a Crime Attack between players
automatically deducts points until one side reaches zero exists to prevent chaos among murderers. If
people just killed each other outright, who would keep playing this game? That said, there are some who
take the risk—usually to steal items."
Nade asked,
"What happens if your points drop to zero?"
"Then all your crimes become known to the world. The guards will
hunt you down. You'll be imprisoned, forced to pay a certain penalty, and then restart at your starting
point. Let me make this clear—don't end up in that situation. You'll be powerless. If you don't have
Crime Points, you can't even fight back against the guards."
Eden asked,
"How many Crime Points are required for magic?"
"That's a tough question. I've heard anywhere from 1,000P to
100,000P. No one shares their point balance, so the details are unclear. But remember this—points are
everything. Combat efficiency between players doesn't exist. The moment you clash, your points decrease
based on your opponent's total."
A single mistake could wipe out all the points they had
accumulated.
"That's why you should only attack someone when you're 100%
certain. And even then, you don't just kill randomly. You do it when your target has expensive items,
when a special mission requires you to kill a player, or when personal desire overrides the point
system."
Curtis reached into his pocket and slipped his Crime Dice between his
fingers.
He had three in total: one eight-sided die and two six-sided
dice.
"When you first enter the inner track, I recommend upgrading your
dice rather than buying items. While items are essential in Melkidu, they're not efficient early on.
Instead, you'll just become prey for the point-rich players."
"I see."
"This is a race against time. If you don't gather points quickly,
you'll be devoured. Anyway, I'm heading to another block now. If you have any more questions, ask them
now."
Shirone asked,
"Why did you help us? According to your own logic, this was a waste
of time. We're not even accomplices."
"That's true. But after running laps on this track for two years,
I've realized that connections matter. As you know, a player's path is decided by the dice. But
precisely because of that, it's important to form as many ties as possible."
One never knew when or where they'd cross paths again.
"Since I own Crime Dice, that means I've already cleared the Dorian
Village mission. The starting point of Melkidu—the first villages that new players jump to—are numbered
from 2 to 12. Most players stay there for a day or two. Veterans are useful, but they're hard to
read."
Shirone's group understood.
"So if we meet again in the same block, there's potential for
cooperation."
"I don't just pick anyone. You guys… you've been through a lot,
haven't you? I can tell from your stance and your eyes when facing an enemy. You can't fool a
detective's instincts. Let's exchange information, trade items, or even work on cooperative missions.
That way, we can all earn more Crime Points."
"Got it. We'll gather the information we need. I hope you find your
daughter."
It was reassuring to know he wasn't a murderer.
"Yeah. May the god of dice be with you. That's a common greeting in
Melkidu."
Curtis pulled out a card with his left hand.
"Wind God, activate."
The card shone and disintegrated, and his three dice rolled across the
tavern floor.
The numbers 3, 2, and 6 appeared, and then two X symbols emerged between
them.
"Multiplication… Did he use an item?"
The three numbers merged into 36, and as that happened, Curtis glanced
back at them.
"Oh, one more tip—get into the habit of closing doors behind you in
Melkidu."
The moment he finished speaking, his body glowed brightly and exploded
into nothingness.
All that remained was his empty beer mug.
Iruki, staring at it, muttered,
"Thirty-six spaces. Even though the dice god wasn't on his side, he
moved far. That means Curtis is now at space 42."
Shirone stood up.
"We can't catch up with our current dice. Well, we'll just have to
start progressing from here."
Suddenly, a system message rang out.
- The tavern owner is watching you.
"Huh?"
They turned toward the counter to find the tavern owner eyeing them with
a suspicious squint.
"Now that I think about it…"
Unlike other non-player characters, the victims of the murderer had
recognized the crimes against them.
"Should we?"
Nade asked.
"We have no choice."
One, two…
"Run!"
As the four of them sprinted for the exit, the tavern owner's eyes
flared with rage as he chased after them.
"Stop right there, you thieves!"
"Aaaah!"
Recalling Curtis's words, Shirone slammed the door shut as they burst
outside.
"Haah… haah…"
Breathing heavily, they turned to check behind them—but the owner hadn't
followed them outside.
"What the...? It's quiet."
Exchanging glances, Shirone cautiously approached the door and peeked
inside…
"Welcome to Dorian Tavern!"
The owner was back behind the counter, smiling as if nothing had ever
happened.
Iruki muttered under his breath.
"A perfect crime."
