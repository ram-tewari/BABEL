6. The Wine of Ilhwa (2)
"No. Even if their lifespan runs out, the citizens of heaven do not die. They are reborn
into new life through the Wine of Ilhwa."
"Then the numbers don't add up. If everyone can live forever, why does the population
remain the same?"
"The lifespans of the citizens are under Ra's control. Strict laws calculate the
lifespans of the entire population, which is how the fixed number is maintained."
Immortality and population control were inherently contradictory.
In the end, the key lay in the Wine of Ilhwa.
But just like Guardrak, Kanya avoided explaining it. Whether citizen or heretic, everyone
seemed to have their own convictions, but when it came to the Wine of Ilhwa, those convictions fell short.
A contradiction.
The Wine of Ilhwa, as Shirone imagined it, was a colossal contradiction.
Passing through the Valley of the Whirling Serpent and the Forest of the Vulgar, they
arrived in heaven.
The group tilted their heads back to look up at the sky.
Compared to the scale of the fortress walls, they were no more than dust. It was a level
of grandeur beyond human creation.
Kanya introduced this place as the 73rd District and added that there were 820 districts
in the First Heaven alone.
Abandoning all attempts to comprehend it, Shirone reached out to touch the wall. He had
assumed it was stone, but it was metal.
The technological prowess of heaven far surpassed the common sense of Shirone and his
companions.
"Is it really okay for us to enter?"
"It should be fine. You're Nephilim. But I can't guarantee anything."
"Then what about climbing over the wall?"
The idea of scaling a wall that reached the sky seemed impossible, but they had no other
options.
However, Kanya shook her head at even that suggestion.
"Nothing can fly in heaven's airspace except authorized drones. You'd be shot down. It's
never happened before, but I heard about it from my grandfather. And sneaking in is impossible anyway."
"Why? There doesn't seem to be anyone guarding the gate."
"They don't restrict entry. It's a matter of the law. All information in heaven is
managed by the angels in the Sixth Heaven, Jebul. They can measure everything from mass fluctuations in each
district to the composition of gases in the air, so even if you sneak in, it's pointless. Don't worry too
much. Nothing dangerous will happen. This isn't purgatory."
As a citizen, Kanya seemed keen on reassuring Shirone, not wanting to plant any negative
impressions of heaven in his mind.
Approaching the gate, they held out a gauntlet, and a red beam of light emerged from a
lens to scan its surface.
The gate opened with a sound that shook the world, a noise that couldn't be blocked even
by covering their ears.
As the entrance to the 73rd District opened, Shirone and his group followed Kanya into
heaven.
The gate closed behind them, and the lens flashed.
The updated information of the transformed 73rd District was transmitted to the Sixth
Heaven, Jebul.
Total mass: 49,730,498,847.3423 kilograms.
Carbon dioxide emission increase: 0.0000024%.

Shamain's first impression was one of strangeness.
The residential areas of different races were divided along the streets, each with
drastically different cultural styles.
The Norrians' buildings were practical, while the Kergoians' were extravagant. And when
they spotted the Mechans' buildings, Shirone and Amy froze in their tracks, speechless.
It was the Magic Warehouse, Istas.
Square containers were stacked in layers, freely shifting positions and intersecting with
adjacent buildings.
It was hard to imagine the Mechans had come here for a field trip to a magic school. This
was evidence that Mecha's technology had somehow flowed into the original world.
Kanya led them past the Mecha district and into a plaza.
The scene in the plaza, where various races mingled like a cultural melting pot,
resembled an international market in a neutral country.
"Shirone, look over there."
Amy pointed at a statue of a giant.
It felt different from the ones they'd seen at the Kergo altar. Here, giants actually
lived. And they had even fought them.
Why would a giant, revered by the citizens, wander purgatory?
Kanya's house was the same size as the other Mecha homes.
If the Kergo sought extravagance and the Norr practicality, the Mecha seemed to
prioritize standardization.
Entering Kanya's house, the building rose to four stories.
Despite its inhuman exterior, the interior was cozy.
Her parents, seated at the dining table, sprang to their feet.
Shirone had heard that the Wine of Ilhwa was for those whose lifespans had ended, but
their faces looked far younger than he'd expected.
Kanya's mother hugged her two daughters and cried out.
"Where on earth have you been? Do you have any idea how worried I was?"
"I'm sorry. There were… circumstances."
"And who are these children? They look like Norrians."
"We went to Norr's Shelter. To get this."
When Kanya held out the potion, her mother's eyes shook with shock.
She had never told anyone about it. How could her daughter know?
"You… this is…"
"It's a stimulant, made with Norr's magic. Drink it before the Wine of Ilhwa."
"Rena! Why would you do this? I never asked for this!"
"So you'd just stand by and watch? You went to purgatory to get medicine for Grandpa,
didn't you?"
"Who told you that…!"
"Grandpa did! That's why he could smile until the end, when he entered the Wine of Ilhwa!
Isn't that enough? That's what family is, isn't it?"
"Why are you like this! Then you must also know that Mom's lifespan was shortened because
of that medicine! How do you think I'd feel if my daughter did the same thing!"
Her father, who had been listening with a somber expression, intervened.
"Both of you, stop. What's done is done. At least they're back safely."
After calming his wife, he turned to Shirone's group.
"So, are you heretics? From Norr's Shelter?"
Kanya introduced them.
"No. They're from the Land of Earth. They saved me and Rena and helped us get this
medicine. We might have died without them."
No parent could remain cold to those who had saved their child.
Her father invited Shirone's group inside.
While her mother prepared a meal, Kanya recounted their experiences in purgatory.
When the topic of Shirone being a Nephilim came up, both parents knelt, causing a
commotion in the living room.
Only after the group's earnest pleas did they agree to treat them normally.
"I see. Thank you for helping Kanya and Rena. But it'd be best for you to leave soon.
Even as Nephilim, those from the Land of Earth won't be welcomed here."
"No. We plan to go to the Seventh Heaven."
Araboat, the sacred land of the gods, was so holy that even speaking of going there could
be considered blasphemy. But Shirone, free from the law, could say whatever came to mind.
"But… there's no way to reach the Seventh Heaven."
"We'll figure that out. More importantly, I'm curious about the law here. What does it
mean for a lifespan to be shortened?"
"Exactly what it sounds like. Citizens are assigned lifespans at birth according to the
law. Kanya's lifespan is 62 years. Rena can live until 73."
"Huh? Rena's the younger sister, but her lifespan is longer?"
"Lifespans can change at any time. Rena gained 10 extra years because she sang hymns
beautifully as a child. It's a good thing, but the problem is my wife. Her lifespan is 43 years and 247
days. Only one day remains."
Shirone's heart sank.
Who in the world could know their lifespan down to the day? The mere thought was
horrifying.
Yet Kanya's mother was smiling. She was confident in Ra's grace.
"My wife lost 20 years of her lifespan as punishment for venturing into purgatory in her
youth. So tomorrow, she'll enter the Wine of Ilhwa."
The idea of altering a person's lifespan was cruel. No, it was hard to even comprehend.
If Ra controlled lifespans, how old was he?
The history of heaven spanned hundreds of thousands of years. Perhaps Ra truly was a god.
"What is the Wine of Ilhwa?"
"Hmm, they didn't tell you? Well, I suppose they wouldn't. It's not something you need to
know. Besides, aren't Nephilim free from the law? It's none of your concern."
"But I still want to know. If I understand it, I might think of a way to help."
"Hmm, well then…"
Just as her father was about to explain, a clear voice rang out from outside the window.
"All shall heed me! I am Peopene, overseer of the Mecha."
Shirone and his group sprang to their feet.
The voice reached them as if amplified by magic. What was most surprising was that the
language was automatically translated.
The ability of telepathy, cast without a channel, using only sound.
If it was magic, it was high-level.
"So it's finally happened. A fairy."
"A fairy? Like the ones I know?"
Shirone thought of the fairies from his original world.
Kanya's father, mistaking his meaning, nodded.
"Yes. The enforcers of the law. If giants uphold the law, fairies are the ones who
execute it. You could call them administrators. She's probably here because of Kanya and Rena."
"What should we do?"
"If you're a Nephilim, you needn't worry. You're free from the law. But… I'm not sure
she'll take that into account. She's… unique."
"What are you doing? Citizens of this house, open the door at once!"
The fairy's voice echoed outside.
They were on the fourth floor. And the fairies Shirone knew couldn't fly.
When her father opened the door, defying Shirone's expectations, a tiny creature flew in.
A girl the size of a palm.
She had golden curly hair, and her delicate features were strikingly clear even on her
small face. She wore green boots that reached her ankles, and her transparent wings fluttered at an
invisible speed.
As Kanya's family knelt, Shirone and his group followed suit.
Peope, hovering in the air, lifted her chin and declared.
"I am the Spiral Fairy, Peope. I have come in person to conduct a citizen trial."
Fairies were born from the essence of lanterns, but they were not of the natural
element—they were concepts.
Singular conceptual entities.
Thus, fairies possessed reason and could dominate the concepts from which they
originated.
"Therefore, I shall now execute the law. Kanya and Rena, have you ventured into
purgatory?"
"Yes. We went and returned."
Kanya's parents clenched their eyes shut.
But Kanya herself remained calm. She had prepared for this since before leaving heaven.
The only problem was… Rena.
"I'm sorry. With my mother's Wine of Ilhwa approaching, I wanted to serve her delicious
food, so I hunted in the Forest of the Vulgar."
The Kergo could freely come and go from purgatory, so she hoped the excuse of hunting
might lessen their punishment.
But Peope, indifferent to the citizens' circumstances, pressed on.
"Hmph, how dare you lie. What use is food to a citizen who will die tomorrow?"
Kanya's mother finally broke into tears.
Even if the Wine of Ilhwa was a blessing, what living being wouldn't fear death? Peope's
words had stirred up those fragile emotions.
Shirone felt anger at that.
The citizens called the Wine of Ilhwa a rebirth. But the words of Peope, an
administrator, were a clear death sentence.
It wasn't mere intimidation, because there was no malice in Peope's tone.
It was like the honest expression of a naive child's feelings.
"Regardless, I shall deliver the verdict. Kanya and Rena, your lifespans shall be reduced
by 40 years each."
Kanya's face went blank. A 40-year reduction meant she had less than 10 years left. Rena
was better off, but even so, she would die at 33.
A 40-year reduction for unauthorized departure was the harshest penalty she'd ever heard
of.
A year ago, her peer Sael had gotten 5 years. Uncle Kai, a Norrian, got 7. Even her
mother, the unluckiest case she knew, only lost 20.
But 40 years? She could accept it for herself, but what about Rena's life?
