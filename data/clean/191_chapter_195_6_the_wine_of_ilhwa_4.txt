Chapter 195:
6. The Wine of Ilhwa (4)
Peope's face froze upon hearing Shirone's words.
She couldn't comprehend the phenomenon unfolding within her heart. As hope for survival
emerged, the intense emotions that had dominated her began to vanish like bubbles.
"Are you mocking me? What do you gain by humiliating me?"
"Mocking? Don't talk nonsense. Saving someone who begs for their life is the natural
thing to do. Because I want to live too. Since my life is precious, I understand how precious others' lives
are."
Shirone hadn't decided to let Peope go. What judgment he would make at the final moment
remained uncertain.
But he thought of Kanya and Rena. He couldn't let them die like this.
"I'll count to three. One."
His grip on Peope tightened.
"Two."
"P-please spare me."
Peope whispered softly.
As if he hadn't heard, Shirone didn't relax his stance. Just as he was about to say
"three," Peope, who had tightly shut her eyes, screamed.
"Spare me! I'm begging you! I don't want to die!"
Shirone released Peope.
Freed, she fluttered her wings and flew up. Her face flushed like a sunset as she glared
down at him.
"Do you think you can get away with this?"
"What did I even do?"
"You tried to kill me!"
"I did. And that's exactly what you did to Kanya and Rena."
Peope couldn't refute it.
Born as an enforcer of the law, she had never deeply contemplated the lifespans of the
people.
But now that her life was in someone else's hands, it was a complicated matter.
Death was terrifying, and life was sweet. Even for a fairy who lived for ten thousand
years.
"I also think what happened to Kanya's mother was wrong. Restore her lifespan too."
Before she could even gather her thoughts, the pressure made Peope snap.
"I already told you! Only the upper echelons of the 72 fairy ranks can tamper with the
Book of Lifespans. A settled judgment can't be overturned."
"Then at least rejudge Kanya and Rena's case. You can do that, right?"
Peope, lost in thought with her chin propped, spoke.
"Well… fine, I'll reduce their sentence to one year."
Shirone let out a hollow laugh.
While it was good that Kanya and Rena's punishment was lightened, the gap in judgment was
too vast. It seemed there had never been any standards to begin with.
"Are you some junk dealer? How can you pass judgments so arbitrarily?"
"This is already me being generous! Should I raise it to 20 years instead?"
"That's not the point. Doesn't this seem wrong to you? There should be standards for
applying punishment."
"That's why I'm the standard. I'm the enforcer of the law! Are you really this ignorant?"
Shirone bit his tongue. He had no choice but to acknowledge that fairies were a different
species from humans, diverging at some point.
Fairy thought processes were more holistic than human ones. While humans could dissect
love into countless concepts, to fairies, love was just love.
Peope was the enforcer of the law, and thus she never doubted her own judgments. Knowing
how terrifying death was, she reduced the sentence to one year.
It was understandable that she couldn't make detailed judgments like "2 years and 3
months" or "7 years and 6 months."
After thousands of years, her thinking might deepen, but currently, she was barely a year
old.
"Are we done here? Then I'll be going. Fairies have a lot of work to do."
"Going where? You still need to resolve the issue with Kanya's mother, don't you?"
Peope turned to Shirone with a glare.
"How am I supposed to resolve that? I wasn't even born when it happened. Besides, my rank
is the lowest among the 72 fairy ranks—I don't have the authority to modify the Book of Lifespans."
"Then at least tell me how. You're a fairy, so you must know something, right? I won't
let you go until you tell me."
Peope, true to her nature as a spiral spirit, was stubborn, but perhaps due to her youth,
she wasn't too difficult to handle. If he could extract information from her, he might find a way to save
Kanya's mother.
"Hmph, do you think you can stop me from leaving? And no one can interfere with fairy
affairs. That's the law."
"I don't know what that law is, but even you know it's unreasonable, right? Life is
precious. How can you arbitrarily cut someone's lifespan by 20 years?"
Peope's eyes welled up with tears.
Truthfully, she didn't know either. To judge the law's right or wrong by personal
standards, she'd need to live at least 600 years.
"Why are you always targeting me? I'm just following orders!"
"Weren't you the one who said you're the enforcer of the law? Where's all that confidence
now? Are you running away?"
"Ugh, seriously! What do you want me to do? What do you want to know?"
"First, tell me what the Wine of Ilhwa is."
"The Wine of Ilhwa? It's a sacred ritual. The laws of the people are refined, and new
life is reborn."
Peope answered smoothly, as if relieved to finally talk about something she knew.
But Shirone sensed nothing but packaged righteousness in her words.
"How am I supposed to understand that? Be specific. What is its purpose, how is it
conducted, what are the ritual's components—things like that."
"Ugh, you're so annoying…!"
Peope clutched her head with both hands.
The Wine of Ilhwa was just the Wine of Ilhwa. She had never reflected on the information
she knew as innate knowledge.
There was only one way left.
Her eyes lit up as she recalled memory transfer.
"If you really want to know, there's a way to explain it properly. Through mental
resonance. Fairies can share memories with others. I'll show you my memory of the Wine of Ilhwa. Will that
suffice?"
Mental resonance was dangerous. Peope didn't seem like she'd do anything malicious, but
she wasn't an ally either.
Arin spoke through the mental channel.
-It's fine. I can handle someone like Peope.
-Are you sure? No matter how strong your defenses are, there's always the outlier factor.
-Peope is emotionally immature, so I can detect the unique patterns of outlier techniques
immediately. Then, I can knock her out with a mental shock before she activates anything.
It seemed Canis and the others had also experienced outlier techniques before.
Thinking about how formidable precognition was, Shirone conveyed his consent to Peope.
"Fine. Show me your memory of the Wine of Ilhwa."
"Alright. Here goes."
Peope cast the memory transfer spell.
As the minds of Shirone and his companions were pulled into the memory, the scene from a
year ago unfolded. It was the square where the giant statue stood, and the people were so quiet they didn't
even breathe.
When Shirone called out to his friends who had arrived at different points, they
approached, passing through the people like ghosts.
Though it was someone else's memory, it felt as vivid as reality.
As a memory entity, Shirone could observe anything he wanted. It was fascinating how he
could stick his face right in front of a person without them noticing.
Shirone and his group headed toward the statue.
As the crowd's barrier vanished, an astonishing sight unfolded.
Multiple hoses were connected to the giant statue, linked to eight glass orbs arranged
around it. The orbs were large enough to hold a person.
The people were silent, but some were crying. The mix of reverence and despair in the
scene was unsettling.
Kergoin scurried around the statue, preparing the ritual. Eight people stepped forward
from the crowd. They were mostly in their 50s or 60s, but one was a young woman.
Shirone saw tears behind their smiles.
Trembling pupils, quivering lips, cold sweat on their foreheads.
They were clearly thinking of death.
The subjects entered the glass orbs, wearing nothing.
As black liquid filled the orbs, their families wailed and rushed forward. Meanwhile, the
other people prostrated themselves and began chanting "Anke Ra."
The families, crying out for their mothers, fathers, and grandfathers, pounded on the
glass, but Kergoin pulled them away.
The subjects submerged in the rising liquid smiled.
Was this drowning?
It was cruel.
But as Shirone peered into the orbs, he recoiled in horror.
It wasn't drowning. Their bodies were dissolving in the liquid.
It was the most grotesque sight he had ever seen.
He wondered what the liquid was made of, and why it was black. If the families had seen
this process, they might have gone mad.
The liquid in the orbs was sucked through the hoses. A disgusting sound, like intestines
expelling waste, filled the air.
The people's chants of "Ra" grew louder.
As the liquid level dropped, the orbs' interiors became transparent again, devoid of any
residue.
There was nothing left.
The people who had been smiling moments ago had vanished through the hoses.
Shirone turned his gaze to where the hoses led. His body was already trembling. Yet, he
had to see.
The giant statue.
There, at the base of the statue, the eight people poured out.
Then, what was it? What was rising inside the statue now?
The eternal Ra.
The chants of "Anke Ra" pierced the sky.
Suddenly, the giant statue trembled violently.
At that moment, the people fell silent.
Not a drop of liquid remained in the orbs. It had all been poured into the giant statue.
Like a mold.
Shirone shook his head. It couldn't be. But once the thought came, he couldn't stop it.
A mold filled with melted humans. Then, what was being forged inside…?
The giant statue split open along a seam.
A massive foot emerged, shaking the ground.
Shirone shuddered at the sight of the giant, coated in a jelly-like substance. Its
expression was too distorted to call human.
Through the mental channel, Arin muttered. The reason he couldn't sense the giant's mind
was that multiple consciousnesses were tangled within a single body.
So, what state were those minds in?
Were they the eight separate minds, or something entirely different?
The people begged for mercy.
Born of the law, bound by the law. The giant.
Thus, the giant had the right to judge those who broke the law.
At this moment, even if someone were crushed under the giant's foot, it wouldn't be the
giant's fault.
People who met the giant's eyes foamed at the mouth and collapsed. It was the eye
technique, Pressing.
Ah.
Shirone trembled.
The origin of Schemata. The primordial Schemata was the phenomenon of human blueprints
overlapping through the Wine of Ilhwa.
Following the giant, he eavesdropped on the people's conversations.
The giant would now leave heaven and enter purgatory. There, it would judge the heretics
threatening heaven before returning to the Fifth Heaven, Mathey, upon Ra's call.
The families cried out in tears.
Mom! Dad! Grandpa!
As if responding to the voices, the giant twitched. But it didn't look back, continuing
its march toward the gates as if nothing had happened.
***
The scenery of Peope's memory burned away like paper, and the house's interior returned.
Shirone and his companions looked around.
Peope was gone. It seemed she had fled during the memory playback.
But now that they knew what the Wine of Ilhwa was, they had no time to dwell on it.
The subjects of the Wine of Ilhwa dissolved in the liquid. Then, they were poured into a
mold, underwent a special transformation process, and were reborn as giants. The people's minds became
entangled, and in that process, Schemata emerged.
That was the meaning behind the awakening drug Kanya had bought.
It was a desperate hope to retain even a sliver of consciousness in the unknown darkness.
Gadrak had been right. It was no different from administering anesthesia to a patient
dying in agony.
"This can't be…."
Tess's face was pale.
The giant's ability, Schemata. And his own ability, Schemata.
When his thoughts reached that point, he ran to a corner and vomited. The food he had
just eaten came back up.
No one blamed Tess.
They all felt sick.
As Rian approached, Tess raised a hand to stop him.
Of course, Peope's memory wasn't more gruesome than a battlefield. But the mental nausea
was overwhelming.
Schemata was the giant's technique. So why could he unleash Schemata? Did he have giant
blood like the Nephilim? But giants had no reproductive organs.
"Now you understand why I didn't tell you."
Kanya murmured with a sorrowful expression.
Some called it a blessing, others a curse.
For this event, debated even among the people and heretics, no one wanted to speak of it.
"According to the law, humans were born from giants. Thus, returning to giants. That is
the Wine of Ilhwa."
Even if Kanya's words were true, it was impossible to accept. The Wine of Ilhwa Shirone
had witnessed was a monstrosity no euphemism could disguise.
At last, he realized.
The Wine of Ilhwa performed in heaven was a ritual that used human flesh and organs as
raw materials to forge giants.
