Chapter 50:
Strange Research Club (2)
Neid immediately showed interest. But their conversation was cut short when the bell
rang.
"The teacher's coming. Let's go in for now."
Shirone set aside thoughts of the club and focused on class. The day ended with the last
lesson at 5 p.m. Shirone packed his bag and left the classroom with Neid.
"Let's talk to Iruki first."
Neid took Shirone to the club. Upon arriving at the magical warehouse Estus, Shirone
stared blankly at the building. If the Estus he'd seen last time was a clustered colony, now it was a
structure with multiple towering spires.
"This is really amazing every time I see it."
"There are 89 buildings, so the patterns are endless. I hope Iruki's awake."
The interior of Estus was as complex as its exterior. But Neid navigated the maze-like
paths effortlessly and headed for the research room.
"I've been curious—how do you find your way? If the structure keeps changing, markings
wouldn't help, right?"
"Hehe. We don't use such low-level methods. We memorize everything. A senior from the
club once restored Estus's blueprints."
Shirone was stunned. It wasn't just about drawing the blueprint of a single building but
understanding the mechanics of a massive, pattern-shifting complex.
Neid pointed to the ceiling of the hallway.
"See those three dots? That means this is Room 3. If it's Room 3, check the color of the
iron door in the third warehouse. It's blue, right? So this place is called 'Room 3's Blue.'"
Shirone's gaze followed Neid's finger.
"Once you confirm that, count the number of warehouses connected to this one. There are
four branching paths, so four warehouses. Now, here's the important part—convert the room number to X, the
color to Y, and the number of warehouses to Z, then plug them into an equation."
"What equation?"
"An equation the seniors created by analyzing Estus's patterns. Input the numbers, and
you get a two-decimal solution. That number determines the direction. So if you memorize this equation,
you'll never get lost in Estus. It's like a mathematical master key."
Shirone wondered if all this effort was necessary for club activities. Estus, with its
immense patterns, and the seniors who'd calculated them all to create a single equation. It made him feel
like there was something extraordinary about this club.
"But wouldn't it take too long to calculate every time the warehouse changes?"
"Only at first. With practice, it becomes second nature. A routine. After about three
months, you'll start to feel it intuitively."
Shirone nodded in understanding.
"That's incredible. An equation as the key to escaping a maze."
"One of the founding members must've had a Servant ability like Iruki. Even Iruki said
the equation was breathtakingly beautiful when he first saw it. Anyway, this must never be leaked. If the
master key gets out, it'll be a disaster."
Shirone was grateful Neid had shared such a significant secret. But it also felt like
Neid was subtly encouraging him to join the club.
A place filled with the unproductive, obsessive passions of its founding members. That
was the Supernatural Parapsychology Club, hidden within the labyrinth of Estus.
After walking for about ten minutes, they reached the research room. As before, the sign
hung crookedly. Opening the door unleashed a cloud of dust, but this time they covered their mouths in
advance, avoiding disaster.
"Iruki, we're here."
Neid spoke without looking, and Iruki, who'd been writing equations on the blackboard,
turned around. Seeing Shirone, he grinned.
"Hey, Shirone. What brings you here? I thought you'd never return to this gloomy place."
Neid burst out laughing, recalling the past.
"Haha! Right. But now he wants to join."
"Oh?"
Iruki's eyes widened in surprise. In his mind, a model student like Shirone didn't belong
here.
"Joining is one thing, but I also came for advice. I thought you guys might be able to
help."
"The genius who defeated me has a problem. It must be something extraordinary."
Now familiar with Iruki's personality, Shirone wasn't offended by the sarcasm.
"After the Speed Gun test, something's felt off. I've been having nightmares, and when I
wake up, I feel eerie. Like someone's beside me. Could this be related to the supernatural or
parapsychology? You guys are experts in this stuff, right?"
Neid and Iruki's eyes sparkled. As explorers and critics of the unknown, they were
fascinated by Shirone's words.
"Then you've come to the right place. Here, take a seat."
Neid dusted off the sofa, sending a massive cloud into the air. Shirone flailed his arms
in panic. He finally realized the absurd amount of dust was from Iruki's chalk powder.
"No, I'll just stand. Please, don't do anything in this room. Or at least clean up
occasionally."
"Now, now. Don't let a little dust bother you, oh first client of our club. State your
business."
Neid and Iruki pulled up chairs and sat with their legs crossed, waiting like
professionals. Though their arrogance was grating, Shirone was desperate enough to speak.
"Well… I've been having these dreams lately…"
Shirone described the dreams he'd been having every night and the strange sensations that
struck him at random.
"I have no idea what's going on. Could a ghost have possessed me? Have you heard of
anything like this before?"
An unexpected silence fell.
Shirone gulped. He'd expected them to joke around, but their expressions were
uncharacteristically serious.
"What? Why are you two like this? Are you messing with me again?"
Neid scratched his eyebrow awkwardly.
"Well… that does sound like something."
"What do you mean? Explain it properly."
"That sensation you described—the eerie, unseen presence. In parapsychology, it's called
'transcendental awareness.'"
"Transcendental awareness? Parapsychology?"
The unfamiliar term made Shirone tense. Neid tapped his temple and gave an example.
"Two hundred years ago, an experiment was conducted on a death row inmate. It was called
'soul transmission'—the idea that if the soul was made of matter, it could be decomposed with electricity.
Here's how it worked: the inmate was sealed in a conductive metal box, and an electric current was passed
through. But it was deemed too inhumane and stopped midway. The inmate survived but was unconscious for a
month. Here's the weird part: when he woke up, he knew everything that had happened outside during that
time."
"That's impossible. How?"
"Exactly—it's unbelievable. The researcher hypothesized 'soul ionization.' The soul was
electrically decomposed, generating electromagnetic force that pulled information from the world to the
inmate. Of course, the academic world dismissed him as a madman. There were even rumors the inmate had
bribed guards for information to save his life."
"Wow. That kind of experiment actually happened."
"Mainstream scholars don't acknowledge it, but it's still a hot topic in parapsychology.
Plus, the Church of Spirits, an official religious group, recognizes trance states. So soul ionization isn't
entirely dismissed. Anyway, that sensation the inmate felt is called 'transcendental awareness' in this
field."
Shirone's spine tingled. The term 'transcendental awareness' connected to his nightmares,
making him wonder if it could be real.
"Now it's time for my conclusion."
Shirone snapped out of his thoughts and looked up. He was curious how the logical Iruki
would interpret this. In this field, the two made a surprisingly great team.
"But first, I have a question. This 'transcendental awareness' started after the Speed
Gun test, right? Your photon output that day was definitely unusual. If it's a secret, you don't have to
share, but it might help."
Shirone agreed. To analyze the result, they needed the cause. Besides, he hadn't planned
to keep it a secret from his friends—he'd just been too flustered at the time.
Shirone detailed his journey through the infinite realm—the epiphany in the sequence of
numbers and the transcendental awakening under the pressure of the duel.
Iruki nodded. He'd heard of the concept from his father. Shirone must've opened a
forbidden door.
"The Immortal Function."
"Immortal Function?"
"I heard about it as a kid. The absolute function that can unravel all the world's
secrets. But in my opinion, it's incredibly dangerous. 'All' is functionally the same as 'nothing.'"
It aligned with what Etela had said, reminding Shirone just how risky his attempt had
been.
"I'd only thought about it—I never expected to actually reach that point. I never want to
experience it again."
Iruki wagged his finger as if to say 'nonsense.'
"Quite the opposite. Like I said, the Immortal Function is already a studied mental state
in academia. The mind isn't like knowledge. Just because you understand it intellectually doesn't mean
anyone can do what you did. Anyway, hearing your story makes me feel a bit better. Well, to defeat me, you'd
have to reach the Immortal Function. Kekekeke."
Iruki's eccentricity, treating even Etela's warnings like a party trick, was unsettling.
But it also strangely reassured Shirone.
"Hmm. So that was the Immortal Function. Got it. That helps a bit."
"Really? You've figured it out?"
Iruki turned his head and stared at the wall as if drawing equations on its blank
surface.
"I'm not an expert in parapsychology, so I don't know much about 'transcendental
awareness.' But those nightmares you've been having—I have a rough idea what they are. And why you're having
them."
Shirone clenched his fists unconsciously.
"Tell me. Anything. I'm ready to hear even the most absurd theories."
"First, dreams distort or exaggerate memories. But your dreams are likely direct
projections of memories. It's called psychological regression—like how hypnotized people recall their past
with vivid clarity. Assuming your nightmares are of that kind…"
"Assuming they are…?"
"What you saw was probably… the universe."
"The universe?"
Shirone knew what the universe was—the vast world beyond this one. But his knowledge
ended there. Or rather, who could possibly know? Since ancient times, humanity had known the universe
existed, but no one had ever seen it.
The only beings to experience the universe were dragons. Six hundred eighty years ago,
Saint Dragon Gramish decided to leave the planet. Before representatives of each race, he ascended toward
the cosmos.
But after just thirteen hours, he crashed back to the ground. His corpse was said to have
been gruesome.
Following Gramish's final words, the representatives divided his remains and returned to
their homelands. The evidence gathered later played a significant role in advancing cosmological theories.
"What I saw in my dreams… was the universe?"
