Chapter 767:
The Republic of South Aymond.
Almareta's expression as she entered the Cabinet Meeting was unusually
bright.
'Is reunification finally at hand?'
According to her spies, Igor was already dead, and just hours ago, the
capital, Pasia, had been liberated.
'How could such a thing…?'
It was only natural to question it. But in the face of a long-awaited
hope to end the war, reason gave way.
The ministers who awaited the President had also heard whispers of the
liberation through their own channels. But few knew the full truth.
"Can this information really be trusted? Something feels off. As long as
we don't suffer losses, fine, but…"
Very few countries in the world had avoided the Demon invasion—but for
these ministers, war had been an economic boom.
'War makes money, after all.'
With death at their doorsteps, the people had no reason to oppose the
Assembly's tax hikes.
"Only one out of the 400 North Aymond spies reported liberation. Aren't
we getting ahead of ourselves?"
That only Jaycee survived meant no one had truly escaped the demons'
surveillance.
"She can be trusted. She's highly capable and deeply patriotic. Igor's
death is real."
The Minister of Interior turned to the Defense Minister.
"What exactly happened?"
At that moment, Almareta entered.
"I'll explain."
The ministers stood, but she motioned for them to sit as she walked up
to the podium.
"It's true that North Aymond has been liberated."
"Do you have definitive proof to say so?"
"Because Igor has been dead for a long time."
The room stirred.
"To get to the point—the central force behind this incident wasn't the
Resistance or any anarchists. It was the Ivory Tower."
A chorus of sighs passed through the ministers.
"I'll handle the briefing."
The Defense Minister stood and moved to the podium, as Almareta stepped
aside.
A 30-minute briefing followed.
"...Currently, it's estimated that there are fewer than ten demons
remaining in the capital. And the two Stars have reportedly departed to seal an Altar."
Shadows fell across the ministers' faces.
"In just one day... Two people liberated Pasia?"
"Monsters…"
What kind of people were these so-called Stars of the Ivory
Tower?
"Thank you for the briefing."
As the Defense Minister returned to his seat, President Almareta placed
a hand on the podium.
"As you're all aware, we stand at the most critical juncture since the
division. The North is in a complete state of anarchy. Every second counts."
The Interior Minister spoke.
"We need to send troops immediately and occupy the North. It was our
land to begin with—we can't just give it away to another country."
Almareta nodded.
"I agree. However, if we act unilaterally, the Ivory Tower will
intervene. We need to reach a mutual agreement."
Though she used the word "mutual," those with experience dealing with
the Stars winced.
'We'll have to beg like dogs…'
The Foreign Minister spoke.
"Leave it to me. I'm confident I can secure non-interference over the
North, even if we have to give something in return."
Calling it "handling" the Stars was a bit of an exaggeration, but the
Foreign Minister had previously pulled off a dramatic negotiation with the Tower.
"No. This time, I'll handle the negotiation myself."
"You, Madame President?"
Unless the Stars demanded it, national leaders didn't usually volunteer
to risk disgrace.
"Which Stars are they? Anyone we know?"
"That's… classified."
Even if they were Stars, they had once been citizens and mages belonging
to specific nations.
Revealing their identities at a ministerial-level meeting would not be
in the country's best interest.
The Foreign Minister, the seasoned diplomat, spoke again.
"Still, shouldn't we at least know the level we're dealing with so we
can properly receive them? How many Stars?"
"Ten."
"Ten…"
The Foreign Minister's brow furrowed as he did the math in his
head.
"Wait—if there are only two of them, how can there be ten
Stars…?"
His expression froze as if it had been carved in stone.
"Ten?"
Basic arithmetic—five plus five is ten—flashed through every minister's
head as they gulped.
"I think you understand now."
As the ministers slowly turned their heads, Almareta gestured toward the
door with her chin.
"Well? What are you all standing around for?"
Shirone and Minerva crossed the skies of South Aymond aboard Jet.
"That's the capital—Gardaan."
A massive city built in the middle of a grassland plain.
"It's bigger than Bashka."
"It's a republic. They absorb more social costs, but their development
speed far surpasses that of kingdoms. Their military power is also leagues ahead."
Though Tormia is considered a global power, South Aymond's military was
said to rival even the Seven Kings Federation.
"I still prefer Tormia."
"Haha. Who's going to argue with that?"
To a Star of the Ivory Tower, nations meant little. But Shirone,
regaining his humanity, was a welcome sight.
"Let's dismount before the city gates."
"No need. Stars of the Ivory Tower are free to pass even the airspace.
Besides, they already know we're coming."
A military procession extended all the way to the government
hall.
"This your first time meeting a national leader? The soldiers are
probably losing their minds right now. They're getting chewed out from above."
"Is this kind of reception really necessary?"
"Not at all. They're only doing this because they want something. Just
follow my lead and don't get swept up."
Crossing over the city walls faster than sound, they arrived at the
government hall. An officer barked sharply:
"Salute the honored guests!"
A grand welcoming ceremony unfolded with flower petals cast by airlifted
citizens, timed with the military band's music.
"Ignore it. Just walk straight."
Shirone, knowing it wasn't genuine goodwill, advanced with a cautious
expression.
Ministers, vice ministers, officials, department heads, generals with
decorated badges—
'That must be her…'
Framed by the government building stood Almareta, President of the
Republic of South Aymond. Wearing a humble smile, she stepped forward.
"Welcome, honored guests. Thank you for visiting the Republic of South
Aymond."
Even under the watchful eyes of the people, she bowed twice to Shirone
and Minerva.
'She's staking everything on this.'
While Minerva smirked knowingly, Shirone was unsure how to
respond.
"Uh, I'm with the Ivory Tower…"
Ignoring the President entirely, Minerva turned to a man in his early
fifties standing off to the side.
"It's been a while, kid."
President of the South Aymond Magic Association, Luda Garcia, gave her a
polite nod.
"It's good to see you again."
A publicly certified First-Class Archmage who had mastered flame magic
to its extreme—his Fireball was said to be capable of burning an entire village.
"Again."
Minerva curled her index finger downward, as if to say bow lower.
"……"
Garcia didn't move.
"Again."
"This welcome ceremony was prepared with great effort. Let's not degrade
the dignity between us."
Shirone had felt it from the moment he first saw Garcia.
'This man is strong.'
His presence was in no way inferior to Lupist, the Magic Association
President of Tormia.
"Again. This is your last chance."
Having forgotten being ignored moments ago, Almareta gave Garcia a
pleading glance.
'Just do it. You know what she's like.'
Garcia bowed a full 90 degrees.
"It's been a long time, Star of the Ivory Tower."
Looking down on him coldly, Minerva jerked her chin toward
Shirone.
"There's one more, isn't there?"
Garcia showed no reaction.
"It's a pleasure to meet you. I'm Garcia, President of the Magic
Association."
"Yes. I'm Arian Shirone."
There was no point dragging the conversation out and risking souring the
mood.
"Wow, you're the Association President now? Feels like just yesterday
you were trembling in front of me."
Minerva's age suddenly felt very real.
"I never trembled."
"Shirone, remember this guy's name. When it comes to flame magic, he's
the best in the world."
The fact that Minerva herself was introducing him gave her words
weight—even though she hadn't spared a glance at the President earlier.
Once she guided them to the conference room, Almareta's expression
turned cold as if by magic.
"May I speak frankly?"
"What kind of direct talk starts by asking permission? Either way, go
ahead. Your sincerity has been duly noted."
It was the bow she had paid to earn this speaking privilege.
"Please grant us governance over the North. We will manage the Altar and
avoid drawing international criticism."
What the Republic wanted, ultimately, was money.
"What do you think, Shirone?"
It would be easier to leave it to South Aymond, but Shirone had thought
the Altar itself should remain under the jurisdiction of the Holy War.
"The seal isn't complete. Are you absolutely sure it won't be
breached?"
"The President doesn't use the word absolute. But I have every
confidence in South Aymond's military strength."
Almareta, casting a furtive glance, pulled a box from under the table
and offered it.
"And this is a small token."
Shirone's face instantly darkened.
"This kind of approach…"
But before he could even finish the thought, Minerva took the box and
opened the lid.
Contrary to expectations of gold or jewels, it was filled with
bonds—each marked with the national seal and their respective amounts.
"At least they're not joking."
"This represents 10% of South Aymond's national budget. Please, entrust
it to me. I'm confident I can satisfy the Ivory Tower."
Shirone's eyes went dim.
'Ten percent of the national budget? That's not a lobby—that's a bribe
we absolutely cannot accept.'
Minerva flicked one of the bonds and tossed it aside.
"This won't do."
'As expected. She is a Star of the Ivory Tower.'
Shirone felt briefly ashamed for having doubted her.
Then came the bombshell.
"Half the national budget. Nothing less."
At first, he couldn't believe what he'd heard.
Even the ten percent they were offered was more than a single human
would need in a lifetime.
'…I see now.'
Almareta looked near tears as she turned to Shirone.
"That's far too much. If you take half the budget, how can we run the
country?"
"What do you think?" Minerva asked.
Shirone nodded calmly.
"If it's half, I agree to it too."
"……"
Once again, as if a switch had flipped, the desperate look in Almareta's
eyes vanished, replaced by cold calculation.
"25%. I can't go any higher."
"We said 50%."
"30%. Any higher, and the Republic would rather forfeit North Aymond
altogether."
"40%. Let's end it here. Two options: either pay 10% annually over four
years, or hand over national bonds now."
"……We'll pay now."
Minerva chuckled, shaking her shoulders.
"You're not bad, Madame President."
She understood well: in four years, another nation could easily outbid
this price.
But if she ended the deal now, the Ivory Tower would never interfere
again.
'In other words…'
Even paying 40% of a year's budget was worth it if it meant securing
sovereignty over North Aymond.
'And with this, South Aymond won't overextend itself. It's best to stop
here.'
What surprised her was that Shirone had realized her intent so
quickly.
'He looked like the idealist type, but he's more pragmatic than I
thought.'
Forty percent of a nation's budget—Minerva couldn't spend that much in a
lifetime even if she tried.
But Shirone wasn't thinking about money.
He realized South Aymond's military budget needed to be reduced.
'They already rival the Seven Kings Federation in power. If they absorb
the North, they'll become a new powder keg.'
Almareta pushed forward the newly written bond.
"You are both in the highest ranks of the Ivory Tower, so I trust there
will be no complications moving forward."
The figure written on the bond represented 40% of South Aymond's
national budget.
