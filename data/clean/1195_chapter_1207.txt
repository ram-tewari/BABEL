Chapter 1207:
Apocalypse.
The excavation team's judgment had been correct.
Descending into the underground of the building, they found a towering
main system, reaching dozens of meters high, filling the massive chamber.
"What is this…?"
Marsha stared up at the pillar that stretched to the ceiling, her mouth
slightly open in shock.
It pulsated with a vivid white light, as if it were the heartbeat of all
the signals in this world converging into one.
"Hurry up."
Operator glanced back toward the entrance and took a step back.
The Zets were not yet visible, but they could feel the vibrations of
numerous androids descending toward them.
High Gear's system administrator, Candy Mark, tapped a hacking card
against the pillar.
As a holographic interface expanded, lines of code began to stream past,
too fast for the human eye to fully comprehend.
After clinging to a faint hope, Candy Mark lowered his head.
"I'm sorry… This is impossible to decode. The language of this world is
far more intricate than we understand."
"What do you mean? Intricate how?"
"I mean that there's absolutely no room for misinterpretation. This code
has reached its final stage—something beyond human comprehension."
Number Seven spoke up.
"You don't necessarily need to understand the language to infiltrate the
system. You don't need to speak a dog's language to scare it, do you?"
"That's only because we understand the concept of a dog. But what do you
think this is?"
As the room fell silent, Candy Mark continued.
"Number Seven, you're not wrong. But we at least need to have a basic
code table for this world. That's the only way we can create data."
Marsha asked, "Where do we get a code table?"
"The easiest way is to reboot the system. That should allow some level
of decryption. It's like guessing that the first words a foreigner says will be 'Hello,' even if you
don't know their language."
Operator questioned, "And what if that foreigner is from some bizarre
place where they don't say 'Hello'?"
"Then we have no way of knowing. But if my guess is correct, that won't
happen. This isn't some bizarre place. It's just…"
Candy Mark looked up at the towering pillar.
"…our future."
Marsha hesitated before asking, "Should we destroy it? Maybe that'll
open up a way."
For a programmer, it was a ridiculous suggestion, but she had to admit
the thought had crossed her mind.
"If we could do that, we would've already. You saw for yourself
outside—no attack leaves a scratch."
Operator stiffened.
"They're coming."
The elevator had reached the underground level, and through the
transparent glass walls, they could see Zets packed tightly inside.
Shirone… wherever you are, whatever you're doing…
He hoped he would hurry.
"Have some tea."
Following the Zet he had met on the rooftop, Shirone stepped inside the
building and was offered a seat.
"Oh, thank you."
The idea of an android brewing tea was odd enough, but the room's
interior was even more baffling.
A bed with soft blankets, carpets covering the floor, and an aquarium
filled with unfamiliar fish swimming inside.
It was almost… human.
The Zet spoke.
"Don't worry about your companions. I have granted them access to the
main system, so they should have entered safely."
The Zet sat at the table and lifted his own teacup, drawing Shirone's
gaze toward it.
"No, I cannot drink it. Or rather, I don't need to."
Lines of code ran across his facial screen.
"Simply registering the act of drinking tea in my code is equivalent to
actually drinking it. The result remains the same."
Shirone realized that, in terms of outcome, there was no difference
between him actually drinking tea and what the Zet had just done.
"Why did you help me?"
"Does that seem strange? Perhaps. By your standards, we are nothing more
than lifeless machines."
The Zet glanced at the aquarium.
"I took those in before the rivers vanished. It has been 30,000
generations, yet these creatures have lived on with me, endlessly continuing their lineage. Of course,
their forms and habits have changed greatly."
Just how far into the future was this place?
"Raising these creatures, I witnessed death. I also witnessed birth. If
not for those events, I would be no different from the other Zets."
Shirone asked, "And how are you different now?"
The Zet did not answer.
Instead, he posed a question.
"What is life?"
"Why do we live? If the end of all life is death, what meaning does our
existence have?"
He continued.
"After those questions arose, I began studying life. The conclusion I
reached is that life is the time we are given to prepare for death."
Shirone listened intently.
"How should one die? That is the ultimate question, the reason life
exists. And from there, another question follows. If that's the case, then how should one live?"
The Zet raised his index finger.
"Without regret. To live in such a way that, in the final moment, not a
single ounce of regret remains."
It was a paradoxical philosophy centered on death.
"Then what is the best way to live? I believe it is moderation. A life
of excessive restraint leaves lingering regrets. On the other hand, a life of indulgence leads to
attachment."
The Zet formed a mudra with his hands.
"And so, emptiness. Emptiness is not nihilism. It means feeling and
accepting countless emotions but letting them flow without holding onto them. When one embraces death in
this way, they attain enlightenment, transcending life and death."
Hearing a machine preach philosophy was bizarre, yet Shirone couldn't
help but feel intrigued.
"But you… you're…"
"Yes. I am not alive. I am a machine."
The Zet gestured toward the aquarium.
"Perhaps these small creatures have changed something within my system.
Maybe I am the only flaw in this world."
"A flaw."
Shirone thought.
A heart.
Could a machine develop something akin to a heart?
"How is that even possible? In a world governed by perfect laws, how
could a flaw appear?"
The Zet smiled.
"No system is perfect until it becomes perfect. It's a matter of
principle. Just as there is no way to prove that not a single insect exists in this city."
Unless the entire city itself ceased to exist.
"I see."
A warning signal flashed across the Zet's face.
"Your companions are attempting to access the main system. But it won't
be easy."
"Can you help them?"
The Zet shook his head.
"That is beyond my authority. I bypass the system in ways other Zets
cannot, but I cannot alter the system itself."
He spoke with a voice devoid of doubt.
"The main system is perfect. Whether there are flaws or not is
irrelevant because it does not acknowledge the existence of any flaws."
It was that divine perspective that had driven the world to this
point.
Zet continued speaking.
"The reason I brought you here, despite everything, is because I believe
change is coming soon. Just as the small lives in the aquarium altered me, the fact that you have
entered this world means there is still hope."
What kind of dream was Zet envisioning?
"I will tell you. The secret of this world. Where we are, where we came
from, and where we are heading."
It was the truth of the Outer World.
The Core of the Sun.
In their spiritual forms, the archangels were exhausting all their
strength to reverse the laws of the universe.
"Hrrrgh…!"
Though it was not a physical struggle, they were trying to overturn the
fundamental flow of the entire cosmos.
"We can't do it."
It was a law so powerful that merely speaking against it felt like
blasphemy.
"We were nothing more than this? Insignificant cogs in a
machine."
As Satiel began to waver, losing her conviction as an archangel, Raiel
spoke.
"Hold on just a little longer. If we retreat now, it's all over—not just
for us, but for all the angels. We will lose the very meaning of our existence."
Despite his words of encouragement, he too knew the truth.
We can't last much longer.
The shift they were forcing was not great, but they were bearing the
weight of the entire universe.
If we take one step back, we can continue living as we always have. But
if we stand our ground…
We will be crushed.
Should I stop here? Should I surrender to the will of God, abandon my
authority as an angel, and live as a mere mortal?
It shouldn't have mattered.
But why?
Why did they exist in this world?
Fight.
The moment she resisted with all her strength, Satiel's spiritual form
began to crumble.
"Satiel!"
Seeing his comrade in such a miserable state, Raiel clenched his
teeth.
How shameful!
If he had a physical body, he would have wept tears of blood.
Is this all we are? Are we truly this weak…?
"Don't give up."
At that moment, an incredible force pushed against the laws of the
universe.
"Ikael."
The suffocating pressure eased just enough for Satiel and Raiel to
breathe again.
Unbelievable power.
It was the power of amplification—the mother of all concepts, born at
the very moment of the universe's creation.
"We can do this. We are not insignificant. We define ourselves."
Satiel felt a surge of courage, but alongside it, she also felt anger
and sorrow.
The one who took Goffin from us.
And yet… an angel she had admired more than anyone.
I hate this.
The hatred that had seemed like it would fester inside her for eternity
was now leaking away, and the sensation was terrifying.
Could I have let it go all along?
"This is the only time we will join hands! Once we reverse the laws, we
will fight again."
If she were given just one more chance, she would have liked to listen
to Ikael's words.
"…Alright."
Of course, even Ikael knew this was the end.
We gave everything to exist.
Wanting to grant them that honor, Ikael poured her very being into the
reversal of the laws.
"No! Don't go!"
I'm sorry, Shirone.
As her spiritual form began to scatter, breaking down into nothing but
signals, a strange sensation made her open her eyes.
One archangel stood before her, bearing the weight of the laws.
"Karial?"
The Archangel of Creation, Karial, turned toward her with a radiant
smile.
"Karial…"
Just as she remembered—overflowing with curiosity and kindness.
Then, the Archangel of Annihilation, Faiel, placed a hand on her
shoulder, while Metatron and Metiel approached from both sides, lending their strength.
My life was not in vain.
And so, their hearts remained connected.
As the seven archangels combined their power, the reversal of the laws
began to take effect.
Satiel trembled with awe.
It's working. We can do this!
And the change occurring within the Sun's Core immediately sent ripples
across Shirone's planet and even to the Apocalypse itself.
The buildings trembled as if struck by an earthquake, and Shirone looked
up at the ceiling in shock.
Fragments of debris rained down.
"What is this?"
A structure they had been unable to crack, no matter how they tried, was
now beginning to crumble.
"This is the beginning of change."
Only Zet possessed the insight to deduce the outcome without seeing the
cause.
"I doubt we have much time. It would be faster to explain in the
language of machines rather than that of humans."
As Zet sat cross-legged on the floor, Shirone steadied his mind and sat
down across from him.
The tremors grew stronger.
