Chapter 33:
Instant Movement (3)
The Uncrossable Bridge was a training ground connecting two mountain peaks at an altitude
of 1,000 meters. The bridge spanned 700 meters, with a raging river flowing below the ravine.
"Why is it called the Uncrossable Bridge?"
At Shironé's question, Aymi explained.
"Because it's literally uncrossable. It's a training ground for instant movement
practice. The difficulty ranges from level 1 to 10. Even for me in Class Four, level 8 and above is
life-threatening."
Siena added, "The advancement test will be set to level 1. No obstacles. For Class Seven
kids, crossing 700 meters is impossible."
Aymi asked, "Huh? Then how will they evaluate it? The first to arrive advances?"
"The student who leaps the farthest will be selected. It's not too dangerous. Safety
measures will be in place, and senior teachers will supervise. The problem is the students. With excessive
competitiveness, collisions among them are common."
Aymi agreed. With early advancement at stake, collusion was unlikely, but Shironé would
still be the primary target.
"How about this? Shironé charges ahead from the start. It'll take a toll mentally, but
someone like him can handle it."
"I thought the same. That's why I'll be overseeing Shironé starting today."
"What? You, teacher?"
Aymi was stunned. A teacher overseeing a specific student was unfair and could lead to
misunderstandings.
"Why would you go that far?"
"I share responsibility for this incident. I initially planned to place Shironé in Class
Five. But other teachers opposed it, and now he's being bullied. Had they listened, this wouldn't have
happened."
"But rumors could cause bigger problems. Other students will study instant movement by
any means, but if word spreads that you're overseeing Shironé, even a passed test could be voided. Let me
handle Shironé for now. I can teach him level 1."
Siena shook her head.
"You've applied for graduation class advancement, haven't you? You should focus on that.
You won't have energy to spare for Shironé."
"Huh? Really, senior?"
Shironé looked at her in disbelief. Being the first to apply meant aiming for a direct
path.
'I see. That's amazing.'
Despite being busy with advancement prep, she'd come to see him. But the mood wasn't as
warm as he'd hoped. The two women refused to yield.
"Even if I'm busy, I can spare time for instant movement. You should back off, teacher.
Your interference will only hurt Shironé."
"As a teacher, I can't ignore a student's critical period. The graduation class test is
only once a year. You should focus on that."
Both wore smiles, but their eyes said otherwise. Realizing no compromise was possible,
they turned to Shironé.
"Shironé, you choose. Me or the teacher? Though of course, your girlfriend teaching you
would be better."
"Shironé, it's best you decide. A student preparing for graduation can't teach as well as
a teacher."
Their voices were sweet, but it felt like a threat. Shironé, however, remained unfazed
and stated his resolve.
"I'll do it alone. I appreciate the concern, but I don't need help."
The women's expressions froze.
"Accepting help from a teacher would be unfair. And I don't want to burden a senior
preparing for a test. I'll pass this test on my own."
"You idiot! The others are getting private lessons! And they're all targeting you! How
can you handle this alone?"
"Then I'll accept that outcome."
"Huh?"
"Senior, you've decided to advance to the graduation class. That's amazing. If I can't
overcome this, I won't be able to face any future challenges."
"Huh? Well… that's true, but…"
"I'll work hard and pass for sure. As your boyfriend, I won't embarrass you."
Aymi couldn't argue further. She was grateful he acknowledged her decision.
"Hmph. O-of course you should."
Though Shironé hadn't chosen anyone, the atmosphere favored Aymi.
Adjusting her glasses, Siena stood.
"If that's the student's wish, I'll accept it. But if you have questions, come ask. That
applies to all students."
"Yes. Thank you for your concern."
Siena opened the door and turned to Aymi.
"I'll let it slide today, but avoid entering boys' rooms in the future. Next time, you
might face disciplinary action."
Aymi bowed apologetically. Even she knew she'd gotten lucky today.
With Siena gone, an awkward silence fell.
For Shironé, it was his first time alone in a room with a girl his age. He couldn't bring
himself to speak.
Aymi, too, felt strange. She'd come to punch him, but now she was nervous around Shironé.
"W-well… I'll go."
"Ah, yes! Thank you for worrying about me."
Aymi sighed in relief and prepared to leave. Then, as if remembering something, she
turned and shouted.
"You idiot! Why didn't you tell me? You should've consulted me if something like this
happened!"
"I'm sorry. But I didn't want to."
"Because of your pride? Fool, what does that matter?"
"More than that… I didn't want to part with you."
Aymi's cheeks flushed.
"Telling the truth would've made things awkward. But I want to keep a good relationship
with you. You're my goal."
Shironé recalled his first day at the magic school. Seeing the name "Carmis Aymi," Class
Four's top student, had made her his goal from the start.
"I'll catch up to you. So work hard too, senior."
Aymi felt both pleased and burdened. She felt similarly but didn't want to admit it.
"Then stop calling me 'senior.'"
"Huh?"
"We're the same age, and I acknowledge you. So don't call me 'senior' anymore."
"But that's…"
"It annoys me! What's with this 'senior' act while secretly chasing me? That's cowardly!
Stand beside me and compete properly!"
In the magic school, class rankings were tied to social status. By telling him to stop
calling her "senior," she was treating him as an equal.
"But hurry up. If you dawdle below, I'll cut you off. Got it?"
With reddened cheeks, Aymi walked to the window. She couldn't leave through the door, so
she had to climb down again.
"Aymi."
Shironé called out, and Aymi turned with a start.
"I'll catch up for sure. Wait for me in the graduation class."
Aymi's eyes curved like a seagull's. Though hidden by her mask, she was definitely
smiling.
"Hmph. By then, I'll have already graduated. Try to catch up if you can."
Aymi jumped out the window. By the time Shironé looked down, she was already gone.
He sighed. Though he'd acted tough, he was at a loss on where to start.
"Instant movement…"
***
A month remained until the advancement test. He had to master instant movement by then.
First, he gathered all the books on instant movement from the library. Contrary to expectations, finding
books wasn't hard.
Rumors said about 20 students were taking the test. Meaning, they were all studying
through special methods, not relying on books.
"I might really fail."
Suppressing his impatience, Shironé diligently studied the basics of instant movement.
The photonization theory, one of magic's greatest discoveries, was a spell that
transformed a mage's body into light.
Understanding light's properties was crucial, but it was too complex for the human mind.
It had both particle and wave properties, no relative speed, and no mass.
Yet, Kergos derived a great insight from this: light's absolute speed could allow rapid
traversal of space.
Anyone could succeed at instant movement by adopting the photonization theory. But the
challenge was controlling it.
Instant movement was too fast for conscious calculation—attempting it could be fatal.
'You can't calculate speeds of 10^20.'
Shironé kept reading. Mages classified light speed into four types: superluminal,
luminal, subluminal, and near-subluminal.
Superluminal, faster than light, theoretically allowed time-slips. But no mage had
reached this unknown realm.
Thus, the greatest light mages were those who wielded luminal speed. They could learn
Stop Magic, a divine-tier spell, but such mages were rare in history.
Most professional mages operated in the subluminal range.
Subluminal was defined as "far surpassing matter's top speed but much slower than light."
It was also the basic ability for spatial movement. Luminal speed allowed instant
arrival, but subluminal still reached the destination in seconds.
Shironé marveled, but it felt unreal. What he needed was near-subluminal, light's slowest
speed.
Near-subluminal was defined as "surpassing a creature's top speed but much slower than
subluminal."
Thus, even if instant movement had light's properties, human eyes could track it.
Without space-warping speed, misjudging the environment could be fatal.
'One missed obstacle is the end. Even with photonization reducing mass, the speed's
impact would be deadly.'
He understood why Siena emphasized no obstacles on the bridge. But she'd said nothing
about collisions among students.
'Can I really do this with just the basic formula?'
***
The time to travel from point A to B was under a second. Calculating variables mentally
in that time was extremely hard. Worse, instant movement required repeated short jumps, increasing confusion
with each attempt.
"Ah, I see."
Shironé slapped his palm. He couldn't calculate mentally, but he could rely on intuition.
