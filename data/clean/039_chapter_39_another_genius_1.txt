Chapter 39:
Another Genius (1)
Shirone advanced to Class Five.
It was fortunate that Siina, who understood his unique "spine of knowledge"
study method, became his dedicated teacher.
Having jumped two classes at once, Shirone sometimes struggled to keep up, but he stuck
to his original approach.
Upperclassmen took weekly tests, with a final exam at month's end to determine rankings.
Shirone ranked 40th out of 40 in his first test.
There were 11 subjects, and his average score was a mere 32.
However, the even distribution of scores between 30 and 33 was noteworthy.
His second theory test average rose to 34.
'A 2-point increase might seem trivial. But...'
All subjects improved simultaneously.
The third test was similar, with scores now ranging between 36 and 39, showing a steeper
rise.
'Everything is rising together.'
Shirone's steadily climbing grades reflected his determination.
'That's a relief. Staying in the lower ranks too long could damage confidence.'
His graph showed no signs of such anxiety, just steady progress.
'Theory tests matter. They're a key criterion for evaluating omniscience.'
Teachers considered 60 the minimum score for basic magic usage.
'If he gains 10 points a month, he'll reach basic magic proficiency in three months. If
so...'
In less than half a year, he could master magic fundamentals.
Of course, humans aren't machines, and aptitudes vary, widening gaps at higher levels.
But his graph sparked a strange hope—perhaps it was possible.
How far could he push his scores without neglecting a single subject?
The answer would reveal Shirone's true potential.
'His relationships are smooth too.'
No one looked down on Shirone for his low grades.
By Class Five, students focused not on rankings but on preparing for the graduation
class.
Countries spared no expense maintaining top-tier magic systems, so students prioritized
specializing to stand out.
Thus, those without clear specialties envied Shirone, a photon specialist.
'Even I think photon affinity suits him.'
Photon specialization favored omnipotence over omniscience.
Though omniscience had advanced theories, human minds had limits in harnessing light's
properties.
Without his intuitive grasp of time fragmentation, Shirone couldn't have completed the
Uncrossable Bridge.
Thus, classmates couldn't dismiss him, and juniors often greeted him during integrated
classes.
Ironically, the junior who admired Shirone most was Mark, back after his punishment.
"Senior, sit here. I saved you a seat."
"Thanks."
Mark, Class Seven's de facto leader, took pride in being close to Shirone.
"Hey! There you are! Is there room for me?"
"Gasp! Hello, Senior!"
Mark jumped up to greet Seriel, a top-ranking Class Four senior beyond his reach.
Seriel, unfazed, plopped down beside Shirone and clung to him.
"Our Shirone seems down lately. Well, your girlfriend left, so it's
understandable."
"Haha! That's not it."
A week ago, Amy had passed her advancement test and entered the graduation class.
