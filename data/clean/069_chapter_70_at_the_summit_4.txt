Chapter 70:
At the Summit (4)
The children familiarly pushed three tables together. Altol, seated at the head, placed
an order.
"First, a round of beers for everyone, and the usual snacks."
Shirone felt a sense of nostalgia. Back when they used to hang out, none of them could
even touch alcohol. But now, they sat cross-legged like adults, chugging their beers. Compared to them, the
kids at the magic academy still seemed like children.
"So, Shirone, can you hold your liquor?"
"Huh? Oh, no. I've never actually had a drink before."
"What? So you're still a kid, huh? You don't even have any hair down there yet, do you?
Pfft—hahaha!"
The children burst into laughter at Altol's joke. But Lumina, the only girl among them,
frowned in disapproval.
"Is that any way to talk to a friend you haven't seen in so long? Honestly, your mouth is
as rough as ever."
Her open defense of Shirone made Altol's face flush slightly.
'Well… she's always been like that.'
He had known since childhood that Lumina had a soft spot for Shirone. But now, none of
them were kids anymore. In this world, the strong won the admiration of beautiful women. Altol believed that
as the future village chief, he should be the one by Lumina's side. Still, his joy at reuniting with Shirone
quickly drowned out any resentment. Shirone, too, was happy to see his old friends after so long. Even the
unfamiliar taste of beer suited him, and the cheerful atmosphere continued.
"Hey, remember when Haulan noona tried to jump Shirone?"
"Hey! Why bring that up now? Cut it out!"
Shirone, slightly tipsy, waved his hands to stop Martin.
"What's the big deal? It's in the past. Besides, Haulan noona got married last year and
isn't even here anymore."
"Oh, really? That's a relief."
The children laughed uproariously at Shirone's response, clutching their stomachs and
pounding the table. As the mood grew livelier, Lumina, emboldened by the alcohol, clung to Shirone.
"So, Shirone, what's your ideal type?"
"Huh? Well… I've never really thought about it."
Altol didn't like Lumina's advances but had no way to intervene, so he deliberately
changed the subject.
"Shirone, so you're really attending a magic academy, huh? Then you should at least be
able to perform simple magic, right?"
"Um… well, the thing is, I started learning magic late, so I'm behind the others. Magic
itself is a difficult discipline, and it's also against school rules to use it outside…"
Even if he could use magic, he was still a student. Without a formal mage certification,
using magic outside the academy was strictly prohibited. The only exceptions were life-threatening
situations or when someone else's safety was at risk.
Of course, most students didn't follow the rules, and the academy couldn't realistically
enforce them, but there were reasons the rules existed in the first place.
Shirone, who had broken the limbs of delinquents in an alley at just 12 years old, knew
this well. Even when Nade used lightning magic in the restaurant, it could have escalated into a serious
fight if the other party had been more aggressive.
Magic wasn't just a tool for violence. It was a powerful force that allowed humans to
harness the energy of nature.
Shirone's specialty, photon output, had no physical force to speak of, but he knew that
demonstrating it would instill fear and awe in the children's hearts. He didn't want to ruin the mood by
showing off magic at a drinking party.
Martin chewed on dried jerky and asked, "If it's that hard, could you fail to become a
mage?"
"Of course. In fact, more people fail than succeed."
"Really? Then what will you do for money if you don't become a mage?"
"Huh? What…?"
Shirone had never seriously considered the possibility of failure. He had poured
everything into becoming a mage.
Making a living was the biggest concern for kids their age, so when Shirone couldn't
answer, they tilted their heads in confusion.
"Then who pays your tuition? The nobles?"
"Yeah. I'm receiving support for now."
The children's eyes widened.
"Wow! Seriously? How much do they give you? Have you saved up? How are you planning to
get married? Have you dated any nobles?"
"No, I haven't thought about that…"
"Then have you been to… you know, those places with lots of women? Nobles go there all
the time, right?"
"Well… some do, but not all…"
The children's enthusiastic questioning made Altol's expression darken. It was as if
Shirone had become the leader.
The hierarchy in the vagabond village wasn't just about beating up weaker kids. Without a
strong leader to maintain order, people's greed for more would spiral out of control.
For them, isolated from society, discord meant the collapse of their community. The
reason they chose a leader from a young age was to prepare for the day they'd have to take responsibility
for the village.
Altol spoke in a dismissive tone. "Enough. What's with all the questions? Are you jealous
of Shirone's life?"
"Of course we are. The nobles are giving him money, after all."
=======================================
"But if you don't become a mage, you're screwed, right, Shirone?"
"Huh? Oh, yeah, of course."
"Relying on someone else's money means entrusting your life to them. If you succeed,
fine, but if you fail, you'll have nothing left. Shirone, you shouldn't do that. Even for your parents'
sake, you should have a backup plan. You can't just mooch off them forever. And it's not like you're strong
enough to work, either."
Even if he didn't become a mage, his education would still help him find a job. As a
commoner, there were plenty of places that would hire him if he swallowed his pride. But it was true that
Shirone had never considered the possibility of failure.
How could he be so reckless? Was it overconfidence? If he didn't become a mage, what kind
of life would he have?
His stomach churned. Failure. Everyone could fail, and Shirone was no exception. The
children's practical questions left his heart cold.
As the mood soured, Altol suggested a game—partly to regain his authority as the leader.
"Alright, enough serious talk. Hey, Shirone, how about an arm-wrestling match? For old
times' sake?"
"Huh? Me?"
"Yeah. You were surprisingly strong back then. Let's see if you've still got it."
The children pounded the table, egging them on.
"Ooooh! Warrior vs. mage! The battle of the century!"
The lively atmosphere lifted Shirone's spirits, and he pushed aside his gloomy thoughts.
Arm-wrestling someone who could lift adults with ease? It was absurd, pride aside.
"How about we make it a bet? Loser pays for the drinks?"
Altol placed his thick forearm on the table, and Shirone extended his hand without
hesitation. When a bet was on the line, a man's pride demanded he accept, win or lose.
"Fine. Let's do it. But no going easy."
"Of course."
The moment their hands clasped, Shirone felt like he was gripping a boulder. He could use
photon magic to flip Altol and get free drinks, but that was just a fun thought.
Martin volunteered as referee. "Ready… go!"
Shirone strained with all his might, but Altol's strength surged like a wave,
overpowering him effortlessly. Shirone's arm slammed onto the table, and he tumbled to the floor, rubbing
his elbow. It wasn't just strength—it was sheer brute force.
"Ow, ow, ow. You're really strong."
"Wow! Altol's the best! Shirone's a mage, and he got crushed in one go!"
Magic and arm-wrestling had nothing to do with each other, but to the children, the title
of "mage" carried significant weight.
"Of course! Altol once hunted a bear!"
Having reclaimed his authority, Altol laughed heartily. "Hahaha! Shirone, you've really
gotten weak. You weren't this bad before. Well, this bet turned out weird. I'll cover the drinks, Shirone."
Lumina helped Shirone up, her eyes filled with apology. "Shirone, are you okay? Honestly,
Altol, must you be so rough?"
As a member of the vagabond village, she knew why Altol had gone all out. But Shirone,
familiar with the life of a hunter, didn't take it personally.
"It's fine. But Altol's gotten really strong."
As Shirone stood, a pouch of gold coins fell from his pocket. The coins spilled out,
rolling across the floor, and the children's eyes widened.
"Whoa! Are these real gold coins?"
"What? Let me see! Wow! Five of them!"
Even the kids at the far end of the table crowded around, mesmerized by the gold. In the
vagabond village, even adults rarely used gold coins. For kids who earned pocket money from odd jobs, the
sight of golden coins was almost sacred.
"You're loaded! Why are you carrying five gold coins?"
Shirone picked them up sheepishly. "I brought extra just in case. There's a book I really
want to buy, and I didn't want to be short."
"Well, books are expensive."
The kids knew Shirone loved books. Even when he'd visited with Vincent before, he always
had one tucked under his arm. The villagers, most of whom couldn't read, had no interest in books. But the
number of gold coins alone was enough to show how different Shirone's life was now.
"Shirone, so you can spend these however you want, right? They're yours, so you can buy
whatever you like?"
Shirone hesitated. He'd never spent money carelessly. As Altol said, it was someone
else's money. But the kids were clearly shocked by the amount. Worried he might inadvertently hurt their
feelings, he chose his words carefully.
"I don't really have much to spend on. Classes keep me so busy I often skip meals."
"Aww. But you can at least pay for the drinks, right? You lost the bet."
"The bet? Oh, right."
Shirone had heard Altol offer to pay, but showing off his gold and pretending otherwise
would only upset him further. Altol, though uneducated, wasn't stupid, so Shirone went along with the flow.
"Alright. I'll pay. I lost, after all."
The children cheered. "Yay! Then we're spending gold! Shirone, can you give me the money
now? I've never even held a gold coin before."
"Let me handle the payment. It's been my lifelong dream to pay with gold!"
"No! I'll do it!"
Before they knew it, Shirone had become the group's leader. Lumina glanced worriedly at
Altol. As expected, his face was clouded with bitterness.
Yet he didn't lose his temper for the same reason as Lumina. Having watched Shirone since
childhood, she knew he hadn't changed at all. He was still the kind boy who put others first.
"Enough! You're acting like kids over a single gold coin! When will you grow up? Let's
talk about something else."
At Lumina's scolding, the children sheepishly returned to their seats. Shirone was
grateful.
Quick-witted Martin changed the subject. "By the way, did you hear? It showed up again in
the mountains yesterday."
"Yeah, I heard. At this rate, there won't be any game left. How long does it plan to keep
eating everything?"
"It must be a pregnant Cowbear. Their appetite is insane."
Cowbears were the largest of the gray bears, and pregnant females ate twice as much as
usual, making them a headache for hunters.
Having hunted before, Shirone showed interest. "A Cowbear came this far? They usually
live beyond the mountain range. That's a real problem."
"Tell me about it. My dad's a hunter, and he's been coming back empty-handed lately. The
game's disappearing, so what can you do?"
"Why not hunt it together? Cowbears aren't particularly aggressive. Five people should be
enough."
"It's logging season, so we're short on hands. Everyone's out cutting trees, so no one's
helping. But judging by its range, it probably won't give birth here. My dad says it'll move on in about a
week."
"I see. That must be tough for him."
Altol's eyes sparkled as he snapped his fingers. "Hey, Bread. What if we hunted the
Cowbear ourselves?"
"Huh? Us? Isn't that dangerous?"
"What kind of question is that? I've hunted red bears, known for their ferocity,
countless times. A mere Cowbear is nothing."
Altol saw this hunt as a chance to restore his tarnished pride. His strength and hunting
skills were his only advantages over Shirone, so this was almost a divine opportunity.
"Hey, what do you say? Let's all help Bread out."
"I-I'd really appreciate it."
Without game, their families would starve. Knowing this, the children couldn't refuse.
Besides, Altol had once hunted the most vicious of red bears.
"Alright! Let's hunt the Cowbear tomorrow!"
"Woohoo! I'm pumped! Bread, don't worry! I'll put an arrow right between its eyes!"
"Idiot! As if! We'll dig traps and leave the rest to Altol."
"We leave at dawn! Shirone, you're coming, right?"
Altol asked with shining eyes. To reclaim his leadership, Shirone had to join.
Understanding this, Shirone nodded, relieved.
"Sure. I've learned a thing or two from my dad, so I'll pull my weight."
"Great! Then let's toast!"
Their frothy beer mugs clinked in the air.

The next dawn.
Shirone woke before sunrise to prepare for the hunt.
The others would bring specialized tools, but basic equipment was each person's
responsibility.
He coiled a rope neatly and hung it on his backpack, then checked his bow and arrows.
He packed cloth and glue for torches, nails, and a hammer.
Spare arrowheads went into a leather pouch, and he even included dried rations and a
whistle in case they got lost.
The familiar weight of the backpack lifted his spirits.
As he opened the door, thinking, "Manly Shirone isn't dead yet," he found the others
already waiting outside, fully equipped.
"Hey, Shirone. You're late. What's this? Heh heh."
Shirone smiled awkwardly. Active hunters were on another level.
"Haha, sorry. It's been a while, so I'm rusty."
"Let's go. We need to be back before sunset."
Altol took the lead, while Shirone and Lumina stayed in the middle, with Martin guarding
the rear.
They reached the Cowbear's territory at sunrise.
Lumina started a fire and began cooking. Breakfast was corn soup with melted goat cheese
and bread. Shirone dunked the hard bread into the soup and gulped it down.
"Delicious. Lumina's cooking is still the best."
"Hehe."
Lumina smiled shyly. In the past, she would've scolded him for not paying for the meal.
The kids had clearly changed—for the better. Shirone was relieved to see his friends doing well.
"Let's head out once we're done eating. I think it's higher up."
Altol led the group toward the mountainside. His guess was correct—the trees bore the
marks of a bear's territory. Martin pointed to a tree with deep claw marks.
"Look at this. The tree's shredded. It must be really strong."
Altol examined the marks closely. "Hmph. No problem. The claws aren't even that big."
"But isn't it still dangerous?"
"Bears are known for their strength and speed. But Cowbears are sluggish. With this many
people, we can take it down."
Shirone studied the claw marks belatedly and frowned. Something felt off. The marks
didn't look like they were made by brute force. Instead, they seemed carved by something sharp and swift.
'Strange. It doesn't feel like a bear's work. More like something fast and sharp slashed
it…'
Altol drew his bow. Crouching with an arrow nocked, he glanced back. "From here on, stay
alert. We'll set traps a bit further up."
The children followed Altol silently, their movements skilled—this was their everyday
life.
Shirone entered the Spirit Zone to sense his surroundings. A 40-meter radius was inferior
to a seasoned hunter's vision, but with his bad feeling, caution was warranted.
As they neared the summit, an unpleasant presence brushed against his senses. Shirone
stopped, trying to identify it.
Altol turned back. "Shirone, what are you doing? We're not setting traps here."
"Altol. Something's wrong."
"What's wrong? I don't feel anything."
Altol dismissed Shirone's concern. No matter how smart Shirone was, hunting was his
domain. But Shirone didn't back down, stepping off the path. Altol chased after him, annoyed. Reckless
actions endangered everyone.
"Shirone. If you keep this up, just go back—ugh!"
Altol froze at the sight before them. For once, Shirone was speechless too. Bile rose in
his throat, the smell of goat cheese souring his mouth.
"What… is this?"
