Chapter 779:
The Definition of Evil (1)
When Shirone and Uorin returned to the room, Minerva had already placed
Lawslayer on the table.
Seated to her right were the four legendary gamblers—Gudio, Agaya, Ness,
and Maice—in that order.
"Feeling a bit more composed now?"
Minerva smiled as if none of it was her fault, and Uorin responded with
a calm smile of her own.
"Sorry. I showed a poor side of myself to my immature little
sister."
Now that she had confirmed Shirone's feelings, she had no reason to let
Minerva shake her anymore.
Something definitely happened.
Minerva, whose foresight far exceeded that of ordinary people, decided
not to dig deeper.
"Let's talk business. As promised, I brought Lawslayer. Now tell me how
you're going to take down the Extreme Evil."
Uorin shifted her gaze to the gamblers and smiled meaningfully.
"We've got some real heavyweights in the room."
There was no way Uorin wouldn't recognize the four greatest gamblers in
the world.
The Ivory Tower must be taking this seriously. These people will be key
to wielding Lawslayer properly.
As Shirone took a seat beside Minerva, Gando poured the tea, and Uorin
took the final seat.
"You've probably guessed already, but the one I'm identifying as the
Extreme Evil is Emperor Gustav Havitz of the Gustav Empire."
The gamblers' eyes narrowed.
"The Gustav Empire has already completed its war preparations. A world
war will erupt within 48 hours."
Several nobles near the border had already liquidated their assets and
fled abroad.
Uorin spread out a map of the world.
"The first flashpoint will be here—the Republic of Cotria."
Cotria, a peninsula nation on the northern continent facing the Pacific,
was practically crushed under the weight of the Gustav Empire.
"Havitz can't expand without eliminating Cotria. Honestly, it should've
already fallen by now, but…"
Minerva added, "The ancient weapon, Ex Machina."
"Exactly. I'll explain more later, but that's the only reason Cotria
hasn't been invaded yet. Once full-scale war begins, though, it's only a matter of time."
Shirone asked, "What's Cotria's official position?"
"They haven't released a statement. They're likely weighing the Holy War
against Gustav, trying to figure out which alliance gives them the better shot at survival."
Minerva scoffed.
"If it were me, I'd go with Gustav. Geographically, Cotria's screwed. By
the time the Valkyries show up, the country will already be ashes."
"I agree. So yeah, they're probably negotiating an alliance in secret.
But the real issue is, if Havitz truly is the Extreme Evil, he'd never accept."
"So the key is Havitz. The sooner we eliminate the Extreme Evil, the
less the world suffers."
"Exactly. That's why we need Lawslayer."
Havitz shattered the balanced three-superpower system. Killing him could
restore it.
Uorin looked around at the gamblers.
"Your task is to design a law through Lawslayer that guarantees Havitz's
death. Change whatever you want—human behavior, emotion, economics, culture, law. The Holy War will
provide anything you need."
"Not gonna be easy."
Gudio spoke up, and the others silently agreed.
"Well, of course not. We're talking about killing an emperor. That's why
we called in the world's greatest gamblers."
"We're not the greatest."
Maice, with his slitted eyes, looked at Uorin.
"You probably don't know how we even got together or why we joined the
Ivory Tower."
"What do you mean?"
The bald old man, Agaya, answered.
"Yeah, we used to be the best. But we all have something in common—we
lost to the same person 14 years ago."
Even Minerva hadn't heard this story before.
"You lost? To who?"
Ness's skeletal face grew darker.
"He's known these days as Gustav's Fourth Art—The God of War,
Balkan."
Uorin recalled the rugged, bandit-like man she'd seen once in the Holy
War ranks—Balkan.
"What was your win rate?"
Gudio answered.
"There was no win rate. We didn't win a single game. No matter the game,
no matter the rules, if Balkan was your opponent, you lost."
It seemed impossible.
"How is that even possible? No matter how skilled a gambler, they can't
defy probability."
Agaya shook his head.
"I don't know. We calculated everything to perfection… but by the time
the game ended, it always felt like we were tricked. Like he was the only one who actually knew the
rules."
"It's not about probability."
The trauma of 14 years surged back into Ness's eyes.
"He reads military qi."
Also known as group aura.
Gustav Empire's royal fortress, Marshak.
Alongside Jincheon's Yeomra and Kashan's Aganos, it was one of the
largest manmade structures in the world.
While every military division was busy preparing for war, Balkan was
quietly playing chess.
Hundreds of chessboards were linked together in a sprawling multi-board
layout.
"What are you doing? Move already!"
The 23 Imperial officers playing against him racked their brains trying
to process the 2,000+ possible piece placements.
No clue. What am I even supposed to move?
A single chessboard had clear victory paths. But when hundreds were
interconnected, it became less about calculation and more about sensation.
It was a level beyond the human mind—probability lost meaning.
"Let's move this one."
After a long debate, they moved a pawn on board 127x278.
Still no idea.
Every piece could theoretically move across the boards, making it
impossible to determine whether the move was even good.
"Foolish choice."
Balkan's voice made them flinch.
"Now that I respond like this, you have no counter, do you?"
He moved a bishop on board 7x16—seemingly unrelated.
What the hell does that have to do with anything?
Maybe after 2,000 more turns, a pattern would emerge. But the sheer
number of possible scenarios made prediction meaningless.
It's not even about calculations anymore. It's wide open
probabilistically. We're only at the start.
So why had no one ever beaten him?
It always ends the way the Supreme Commander says it will.
Balkan was blessed by both the gods of wisdom and war. The officers
stared at him with awe.
What does he see… in all this?
From the edge of the interconnected boards, Balkan sat cross-legged and
overlooked the battlefield.
There. That's the weak point.
To his eyes, golden threads of military qi surged through the
chessboard.
He directed his subordinates to move their pieces toward the dimmest
thread of light.
"Weak decision."
While a supercomputer could calculate the layout, military qi didn't
follow logic.
It'd be easier to quit.
Military qi rejected probability.
It didn't care about formations—it followed the shifting intent of the
crowd.
"Let's stop here. You've already lost."
From the officers' placements, the qi conveyed one thing: reverence for
Balkan himself.
"What about the Emperor?"
"Oh, I heard he's out for a walk…"
"So boring."
Balkan yawned.
"Why is today so damn long?"
Back in the strategy room, Shirone asked, "Is he really that difficult
an opponent?"
The gamblers all responded in unison.
"He is."
Agaya raised a finger.
"Military qi isn't about objective probability. It's the apex of
reactive adaptation. In a game with an opponent, no one can win against that. If someone like that is
backing Havitz, even Lawslayer might not be enough."
"But at the same time…"
Ness said, "That's exactly why he's worth beating. This time, we'll win.
We have to."
The room burned with renewed resolve.
"Alright," Uorin said. "Let's begin the analysis. First, we need to know
exactly who our enemy is."
Uorin looked back at Shirone.
"Extreme Evil. Have you ever met one in your life?"
"Hmm, I'm not sure? I've met plenty of bad people, but…"
Minerva pointed at herself.
"Me?"
"You're not Extreme Evil."
Uorin shook her head.
"Sure, Minerva, you're definitely a terrible person—but Extreme Evil
isn't something that can be described in such simple terms."
Minerva pouted her lips.
"So, you probably won't want to hear this, but…"
This is a story.
"A story you'll want to look away from for the rest of your
life."
A tale of a person most removed from humanity—of the Extreme
Evil.
"Please! Help me! My husband's dying!"
A woman screamed into the street in the commoner district of Marshak,
the imperial capital of Gustav.
"What's going on?"
A man in his fifties came running up, dressed as a clown with a drum
strapped to his back.
"Ah… uh…"
The woman, hoping for help, frowned when she saw the man's peddler-like
costume.
"Someone help us! My husband…!"
She didn't recognize the face of Zetaro, one of the Four Banners of
Gustav—the Merchant of All Things.
"Then speak to me! I may look like this, but I've studied medicine! A
life should be saved, shouldn't it?"
Zetaro was the most brilliant doctor in the empire.
"Can you really save him?"
It wasn't something one would approach without confidence, so the woman
decided to trust him.
"Here, over here! He suddenly collapsed and started foaming at the
mouth!"
Her husband lay on the wooden floor, while her young daughter and son
cried at the stairs.
"Let me have a look first."
Zetaro checked the husband's complexion and pupil response, immediately
diagnosing the condition.
"The Baskia virus strain. Has he been to a lake in the last two
months?"
"What? Ah—yes! He went fishing with friends!"
Zetaro rummaged through his merchant's bag and pulled out a small bottle
filled with a brown liquid.
"This medicine…"
"Will that save him if he drinks it?"
"No, this is for me. I've got a bit of a condition."
The woman watched in disbelief as Zetaro uncorked the bottle and gulped
it down himself.
"Please! Save my husband! You can save him, right?"
"The Baskia virus, huh… The cure would be…"
As he hesitated with a scalpel in hand, Zetaro suddenly gritted his
teeth and let out a furious scream.
"How the hell would I know that?!"
Veins bulging in his eyes, he stared at the ceiling and began stabbing
the man's chest with the scalpel.
"KYAAAA!"
The woman screamed as blood sprayed across the room. Her children passed
out, eyes rolled back in terror.
"W-What… What the hell are you doing…?"
Having already taken a vaccine for the virus, Zetaro, now soaked in hot
blood, calmly walked outside.
"Hey, Zetaro. I'm starving. Wanna grab a bite?"
Havitz in ragged clothes and Natasha in a suit approached, trailed by
imperial guards.
"Your Majesty, another life has been lost. So very, very tragic…"
Overcome with unbearable grief, Zetaro couldn't even finish his
sentence.
"I shall offer a song."
Hopping on his left foot and slapping the drum with his right, he pulled
out a trumpet and blew a tune.
"Turubapbadoo. Turubapbapboo."
The trumpet of the empire's greatest minstrel echoed through the street,
stirring the hearts of bystanders.
"You bastards! You murdered my husband, and now you're—!"
The woman came rushing out of the house with a knife, but the guards
quickly slammed her to the ground.
"Aaaaah! It's the Emperor!"
Only then did the mesmerized crowd realize what had happened and begin
to scatter.
"Puhahaha! Puhahaha!"
Havitz, finding the whole situation hilarious, clutched his stomach and
roared with laughter.
