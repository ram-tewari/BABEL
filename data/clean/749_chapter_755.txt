Chapter 755:
The Holy War was spiraling toward its worst outcome.
Even after Gustav Havitz XVII declared war on the world and stormed out
of the chamber, none of the world's leaders could find the words to speak.
Is this really happening? Are we truly going to war?
Even these leaders—standing at the very peak of global power—couldn't
calm their pounding hearts. After all, their opponent was the most fearsome tyrant in human
history.
President Basari of the Holy War host nation, the Republic of Courtria,
spoke with a pale face.
"Are we supposed to believe this? That a world war just began… right
now?"
If Havitz mobilized his forces, Courtria, closest to the Gustav Empire,
would be the first target.
"Analyzing Havitz's thoughts is impossible. But if he's staying true to
his desires, then yes—it's likely real."
Even among the Holy War delegates, Uorin's words carried particular
weight. President Basari looked like he was on the verge of tears.
"But why? Isn't this a war he can't even win? The Holy War will
suffer—but the Gustav Empire will be annihilated."
King Kasartak of the Kingdom of Paras spoke up.
"Our internal assessment places Gustav's chances of victory at under ten
percent—assuming they've truly abandoned the Valkyries. If he's just some lunatic, we can crush him and
be done with it."
"He's not insane."
Everyone turned to Uorin.
"Of course, Gustav's chances of victory are extremely low. But if we
account for a certain scenario—those odds shoot above 90 percent."
Emperor Jingang of the Jincheon Empire asked, "And what's that?"
"The probability that the world collapses into total chaos."
The pupils of every representative shook.
"Havitz isn't just thinking about war. If the Gustav Empire marches
east, south, and across the continent, maintaining the Spiritual Barrier will become impossible.
Eventually, the demons will break through the barricades and pour into the world…"
From that point on, it would be chaos.
"He's pushing us into an unpredictable situation," someone said.
Uorin nodded.
"Havitz moves by instinct—but the Four Great Arts of Gustav are probably
already drawing up a plan. If probability no longer matters, then it becomes worth trying. And in such
conditions, Havitz's irrational way of thinking becomes a terrifying weapon."
If war consumes all available resources, then Gustav's strategy of
utilizing the Under World was disturbingly effective.
Havitz XVII… he's terrifying. What's worse is that he doesn't think—he
simply feels.
And when one truly believes, the world inevitably begins to revolve
around that belief.
"Is that the end of the human discussion?"
Blitz, the First Dragon, rose from his seat.
"We've stayed long enough out of formality as members of the Holy War.
Now, I will present the position of the dragon race."
His tone was arrogant—but no one took offense. Blitz had earned that
right.
One of the Twelve Apostles who Guard Time. And the most powerful in
battle—the Thunderwing Dragon.
This was the first time he had ever attended the Holy War as a
representative, and the room sharpened its focus.
"As of today, the dragon race withdraws from the Holy War."
"……"
Anyone could withdraw—provided they were willing to pay the price.
Still, a low murmur ran through the room.
"May I ask why?" someone said.
It wasn't yet time to reveal the full truth to humanity.
"The dragon race joined the Holy War to preserve time in the face of
Anke Ra's reset. But now, Ra's will has passed to Nane, and the closing of time is imminent. To us,
Emptiness must be stopped before Evil."
With the emergence of Love and Emptiness as new polarities, the world
could no longer be defined simply by good and evil.
So it's come to this…
Nane, who believes the truth lies where the real world ends—and Shirone,
who believes truth is found in where we choose to invest our hearts.
This is just the beginning. Many will soon define themselves according
to the compass of Law.
Some nations would align with the Gustav Empire. Even Holy War member
states would not be exempt.
Enox, representative of the fairy race, asked,
"You're leaving the Holy War? Then what about us?"
"Why ask me? You're the fairy representative. If you want to side with
the humans, go ahead."
Though it sounded dismissive, it also made clear the dragons' reluctance
to meddle in human affairs.
"Hmph…"
While the Seven Kings' representatives watched with grim expressions,
Uorin alone was confident.
They'll stay in the Holy War.
Fairies of Heaven and the fairies of the mortal realm are classified
differently—as Fairies and Elves.
Elves are as mentally resilient as fairies and possess bodies closer to
humans.
Their command of magic is far beyond human capability.
They're thought to be a hybrid of Heaven's fairies and the Norin, which
suggests a horrific ancient experiment.
"I'll remain in the Holy War," Enox said. "If war breaks out, so many
lives will be lost. Of course the fairy race must help."
Uorin felt a chill.
What kind of race risks their lives for those weaker than
themselves?
If this is goodness, it was a deeply "artificial" form of it.
"O Representative of the Fairies, your choice has warmed the hearts of
all gathered here."
Blushing, Enox stuck out her tongue.
"Hehe, I got praised. Don't worry. Fairies have always helped humans.
We're not like the noble dragons."
"Hmph."
Blitz turned coldly and, before even exiting through the door,
transformed his body and soared skyward.
He crashed through the ceiling, and chunks of stone fell onto the round
table below.
"…This is the worst Holy War yet."
Shirone tucked the dagger into his robe.
This can't be replicated.
Material can theoretically create anything because it reconstructs
matter.
But Lawslayer's essence wasn't in its physical form, but the spirit
embedded within it—something no replication could mimic.
All I'd be making is a dagger with the same shape.
It was like synthesizing organic compounds to create a human body—but
still being unable to breathe life into it.
Now that Verdie had calmed down, her mother stood.
"We need to clean the house."
Even for her, the sight of the corpses was horrifying—but if they
weren't careful, the whole family could be executed.
"I'll go rinse a rag. We need to get the blood first—"
"It's no use, Mom."
Verdie shook her head.
"They'll find out anyway. They know exactly which houses owe
collections. If we don't run now, we'll be caught."
No one would believe a story about Lawslayer.
What they'd face wouldn't be execution—it'd be worse: endless torture
designed to uncover the truth.
"You don't have to worry."
Shirone stepped toward Verdie and her mother.
"You don't have Lawslayer anymore, but I'll protect you. So let's think
of a plan—together. What do you think we should do?"
For a moment, Verdie's eyes lit up with hope—but they quickly dimmed
with despair.
"Don't lie. Why would someone as strong as you help people like us? Mom
and I are useless."
She knew perfectly well that Shirone had come for Lawslayer, not
her.
"You're going to leave eventually. You'll say you don't have time, or
that you've done enough, or you'll just get tired of it…"
Raised in such a harsh world, she seemed to know more than any adult
raised in peace.
"I won't leave."
Shirone stroked her head with a gentle, sorrowful smile.
"Not until every threat to your family is completely gone. I
promise."
If love means not being able to kill one to save another—then Shirone
had to resolve this.
"You really… will protect us?"
The burden of having to protect everyone was painful—but the simple fact
that he wouldn't leave right now made her happy.
"Of course. You're never a burden. You're the most precious person in
the world. I'll do everything I can to protect you."
Verdie and her mother, who had never heard such words in their lives,
burst into tears.
"Thank you. Thank you so much."
With little time to spare, Shirone regained his composure and turned to
Verdie's mother.
"Is this your entire family? Your husband…?"
"No. My husband is doing forced labor right now. He works at a military
facility for about a month at a time, then comes back."
A shadow fell over her face.
"No matter how much I ask what kind of work it is, he won't say. He says
if we find out, our whole family will be killed. But…"
"But?"
"I don't think it's normal. When he comes home, he doesn't eat for three
days straight—just sleeps. And he has strange nightmares. He says weird things in his sleep."
"What kind of things?"
She hesitated, unsure if she should even say it aloud, but eventually
confessed.
'"I won't give birth to a monster's child,"' she said.
"Forgive me for asking, but your husband is…"
"Yes, of course, he's a man. Verdie's biological father. But isn't that
strange? When I asked him about it in the morning, he got angry and threw things. He's never once raised
his voice at his family."
Shirone had a few ideas, but this wasn't the time or place to share
them—not in front of Verdie and her mother.
"Alright. Let's go find your husband."
Leaving the soldiers' corpses where they were, Shirone led Verdie and
her mother out of the house.
Even with his hood up, suspicion would come quickly, so they made a wide
detour through the mountains and approached the military facility from behind.
This place is on a whole other level.
Shirone had visited many nations while sealing Altars, but nowhere had
the same strict military discipline as here.
"Are you sure it's okay for us to follow you?"
Verdie and her mother clung close to Shirone, so nervous they could
barely breathe.
"I don't know what your husband looks like. If you see anyone familiar,
please let me know—even if it's not him."
They passed by about seven buildings, avoiding the main patrols, when
Verdie's mother pointed ahead.
"There—that's Mr. Albas."
A man dragging a cart that reeked of rotting food looked barely alive,
his eyes completely lifeless.
"He's a friend of my husband's. My god… how could someone change this
much…"
Just a month ago, the Albas she remembered had been a burly man weighing
over 100 kilograms.
"Can you call out to him?"
Wanting desperately to find her husband, Verdie's mother stepped out
from the shadows and summoned her courage.
"Mr. Albas! Mr. Albas!"
Albas didn't even turn his head at first, but then snapped to attention
and turned around with a shocked expression.
"Mardel? What are you doing here…?"
Dropping the cart handle, he glanced around and quickly ran over,
gripping her by the shoulders.
"Are you crazy? Do you know where you are? Get back home, now!"
"My husband! Have you seen him?"
Only then did Albas notice Verdie clinging to her mother's hand—and the
stranger standing beside them.
"Unbelievable… don't tell me you came to rescue him?"
"That's right. This man is going to protect us. You should come with us
too, Mr. Albas."
Albas lowered his head with a bitter expression.
"Don't bother. I don't know who this guy is, but Baseto isn't coming
back. Just take care of Verdie. Tell my family the same thing."
"What do you mean he's not coming back? Don't tell me he's…!"
Shirone cut her off.
"What kind of work are they doing here?"
Albas had likely numbed himself to survive this long, refusing to think
at all.
As he searched for the right words, a realization hit him—and his face
turned pale.
"I don't know. I really don't."
Albas covered his face with his hands and sobbed.
"This place… a devil lives here."
