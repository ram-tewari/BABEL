Chapter 786:
Mitochondrial Eve is powerful in that she can transmit an individual's
experiences to the next generation with one hundred percent accuracy.
Even before they gained the name Terraje, they had been dividing like
cells, absorbing the histories of the world as they spread.
They built a vast database by integrating all that experience and
passing it on to the next generation.
History Search.
Through this, Uorin was able to locate the first encounter between
Havitz and Abella.
"Havitz meeting her is what changed Abella's fate."
As Uorin explained, a gentle smile formed on Abella's lips, lost in the
memory.
"Playing with Mister Havitz was really fun. He always knew what I
liked—unlike the other adults."
He was now known as the most evil man in the world, but no one could
outright deny her words.
"I know about the witch's fate. People told me about it ever since I was
born."
The Yus tribe was a gypsy people who understood the Law.
"If Mister Havitz hadn't broken my cursed fate, I'd probably be living
in pain right now as a witch."
The story returned to fourteen years ago.
"Let's dig through here and make an underground canal. Big enough for a
monster frog to pass through."
On the sandy shore of the fishing spot, Havitz and Abella were focused
on building a sandcastle.
His baitless fishing rod had been cast into the sea for over ten days
now, but the shark he wanted still hadn't shown.
"You know a lot about castles, mister."
"Of course I do. It's my house."
Abella, flattening the ground with her hands, looked up with a face full
of sand.
"Mister, are you the emperor?"
"Hm? The emperor?"
"You said your name is Havitz. The adults say the emperor of this
country is named Havitz."
"Oh, that's Havitz the Sixteenth. That's not me."
"Is it okay to have the same name as the emperor?"
"Hmm, I guess so? I know hundreds of people named Havitz."
Abella muttered, disappointed.
"It'd be nice if you were the emperor."
Having delicately finished the underground canal, Havitz asked a bit
later,
"Why the emperor? Doesn't sound very fun."
"Because you can do anything. My mom says if I want to change my fate,
I'd have to be someone like the emperor."
"Fate? What's that?"
It was after ten days of playing that Abella finally brought up the
darkness in her heart.
"People in my tribe say I'll become a witch. My mom's a witch too.
That's why she always has to give her body to men."
The Yus tribe was in decline.
"Back then, the Yus were respected for their Law, and people came from
far away to get their fortunes read. That's how we used to overcome the witch's fate. But now they say
that time has passed."
The tribespeople couldn't let the name of Yus, which had thousands of
years of history, die out with their generation.
"Now we can't tell fortunes anymore. So the girls with the witch's fate
go around selling their bodies."
In Bardol, the village where the Yus tribe now lived, there were both
fishermen and pirates.
There wasn't much difference between them.
"I don't want to go back. The adults keep coming and touching me. My mom
got mad once, and the men—way bigger than you—beat her nearly to death."
"So being a witch isn't fun, huh?"
"Of course not. What's good about it? Just thinking about it is
disgusting. I want to grow flowers."
"Then don't be a witch."
Abella shook her head.
"They say you can't change the Law. Every night, my mom holds me and
cries. She says she's sorry. But she still says I'll have no choice but to become a witch."
"I don't get it."
"Well, of course you don't. Only gypsies understand."
"If you don't want to be a witch, just don't be one. That's all."
Perfect chaos.
To Havitz, who lived entirely outside the rules of "how things should
be," the Law was nothing but an empty concept.
"Just do what you want. Life's more fun that way. Why do boring
things?"
"...Can I really?"
Deep in thought, Abella smiled brightly.
"But I'm having fun now that I play with you. Before, I was always alone
in the forest."
They completed a beautiful sandcastle.
"I'm having fun too. No one in the palace ever wants to play. They just
cry at everything."
Havitz mimicked the frightened faces of the people around him, and
Abella laughed until her stomach hurt.
"Haah, I wish I could play with you every day."
"Then let's play every day."
Abella hesitated.
"But you're an adult. Mom said adults are busy, so I shouldn't bother
them."
"It's fine. Playing with you is the most fun. I'll just not do the other
stuff. Who cares?"
Abella's eyes blinked sweetly.
"You're really different from the adults I know."
"How so?"
"You don't tell me what to do or not to do. You don't act like I'm
annoying."
"Other adults do that?"
"More than you can imagine. 'You have to be a good person,' 'Hold your
spoon like this'... they never stop."
Havitz twisted his mustache.
"Hmm, I wouldn't know. I've never heard things like that in my
life…"
And so he came to a conclusion.
"People who say things like that are scared. Adults are always scared,
as far as I can tell."
"Scared of what?"
Havitz shrugged.
"Pfft, who knows? What's there to be afraid of in this world? It's like
they think they're walking on a tightrope over an invisible cliff."
He made walking motions with two fingers.
"But when I tried walking around, there was no rope at all. You don't
fall no matter where you go. It's obvious. But people keep pretending they don't know."
"Hmm. So, you don't fall."
"If you want to play with others, you should give them what they want
sometimes. But if you don't want to play, there's no need to listen. It just gets a little boring,
that's all…"
Havitz comforted her.
"As long as you're not afraid, you can do anything. You should grow
flowers. Don't listen to all that boring stuff about witches."
She didn't understand all of his words, but it was the most comforting
thing anyone had ever told her.
"Okay! I'm going to grow flowers starting today! Thank you,
Mister!"
Havitz grinned, baring his teeth.
From that day forward, Havitz and Abella would go into the forest and
collect flowers every time they met.
One flower a day.
They didn't know the names or species, but just doing what they wanted
was enough to make them happy.
Still, the shark showed no sign of appearing.
"Maybe I should go farther out."
Havitz sat by the fishing spot, yawning as he looked out over the
endless sea.
"Why's she so late today?"
Abella had never been late before—but today, she hadn't shown up.
"Maybe she doesn't want to play with me anymore?"
Missing her, Havitz stood up and headed toward her village.
From the moment he reached the fishy-smelling entrance of the fishing

"Damn gypsies! We fed you, sheltered you, and this is how you repay

Hexter, the village leader, kicked a frail woman to the ground as Abella

"Waaaah! Mom!"
"Abella! Why did you come?! I told you to run!"
Abella shook her head.
"No! I'm not leaving you behind!"
It had been two months since the gypsies settled in the village. The

Her mother resisted desperately, leaving claw marks on Hexter's face—and

"People who couldn't even find a place to rot anywhere else dare lay a

"Mom! Mom!"

"Abellaaa~. Let's play~."
